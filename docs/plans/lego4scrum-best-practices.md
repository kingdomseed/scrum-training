# Lego4Scrum Best Practices: Research from Experienced Trainers

**Date:** 2026-03-06
**Purpose:** Research-only report — practical tips from experienced Scrum trainers who have run Lego4Scrum and similar Lego-based Scrum simulations, tailored to our 2-day workshop for 6-8 non-technical art education staff.

---

## 1. Top 10 Facilitation Tips (Ranked by Impact)

### 1. Keep Acceptance Criteria Hidden — Make Teams Ask
The single most impactful technique across all sources: the PO has "secret" acceptance criteria (symmetry, color consistency, windows/doors, proportional scale) but does NOT volunteer them. Teams must learn to ask. This is the core teaching moment — people don't ask questions and just start building based on assumptions.
- Sources: [Agile Parrot](https://agileparrot.com/agile-workshops/lego-scrum/), [Lego4Scrum Blog](https://www.lego4scrum.com/blog/)

### 2. Sprint 1 Must Fail — Protect the Chaos
Do not intervene during Sprint 1 build. Let teams overcommit, build independently, skip questions, and produce inconsistent work. The learning happens in the contrast between Sprint 1 and Sprint 3. Every experienced trainer confirms: "No team has failed to understand this by the start of the third sprint. Two sprints of failure really drive the point home."
- Sources: [Agile Parrot](https://agileparrot.com/agile-workshops/lego-scrum/), [Velthoven Consultancy](https://www.velthovenconsultancy.nl/agile/scrum-simulation-lego)

### 3. Separate Trainer Hat from PO Hat
Explicitly tell participants when you're speaking as trainer vs. Product Owner. This prevents confusion and models how the PO role works. Say: "OK, I'm taking off my trainer hat now. I'm your Product Owner. Here's my vision for this campus..."
- Source: [Velthoven Consultancy](https://www.velthovenconsultancy.nl/agile/scrum-simulation-lego)

### 4. Use a Visible Countdown Timer
Every source emphasizes visible timekeeping. Use a large phone/tablet timer or projected timer. This reinforces timeboxing as a Scrum principle and creates urgency. End sprints precisely at 7 minutes — "everyone raise your hands!" — to enforce the discipline.
- Sources: [Agile Strides](https://agilestrides.com/training/lego-simulation-scrum-workhop/), [Agile Parrot](https://agileparrot.com/agile-workshops/lego-scrum/)

### 5. Be a Storytelling PO, Not a Checklist PO
Frame requirements through narrative and emotion, not dry lists. "I need a place where children can play safely — parents want to know their kids are safe in our campus" is far more powerful than "Build a playground, minimum 8 bricks." This teaches teams to understand WHY, not just WHAT.
- Source: [LinkedIn - Pascal Ogil](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/lego4scrum-how-pascal-ogil)

### 6. Accept All Ideas That Fit the Fiction
When acting as facilitator/narrative guide, accept participant suggestions that don't contradict the established theme. If someone asks "Can our gallery have a rooftop sculpture garden?" — say yes. This builds confidence and ownership, especially important for non-technical participants who may feel uncertain.
- Source: [Carl J Rogers - Medium](https://medium.com/@carljrogers/lego4scrum-how-to-build-shared-narratives-that-learners-will-love-triple-loop-agile-882d241cfb22)

### 7. Don't Minimize the Facilitator Role
"Don't minimize your role in the game — you have to facilitate, coach, and maintain the rhythm." The facilitator must actively manage energy, transitions, and timing. This is not a "set it and forget it" exercise.
- Source: [LinkedIn - Pascal Ogil](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/lego4scrum-how-pascal-ogil)

### 8. Track Velocity Visibly
Update a burndown chart or velocity tracker on the wall after each sprint. This creates a powerful visual of improvement and gives teams data for better planning in subsequent sprints. Even a simple "points accepted" tally per sprint works.
- Sources: [Velthoven Consultancy](https://www.velthovenconsultancy.nl/agile/scrum-simulation-lego), [Agile42](https://www.agile42.com/en/agile-teams/scrum-lego-city)

### 9. Require Actionable Retro Items
Insist retrospective outputs are concrete actions, not vague sentiments. "We should communicate more" is not an action. "We will show the PO our work halfway through the next build" is. If retros produce only vague items, the retrospective is a waste of time.
- Source: [Agile Strides](https://agilestrides.com/training/lego-simulation-scrum-workhop/)

### 10. Consider "Unexpected Event" Cards for Sprint 3
If teams are doing well by Sprint 3, inject a disruption: "A team member is called away for 2 minutes" or "The PO has changed priorities — the amphitheater is now urgent." This teaches adaptability and keeps energy high. Only use if teams are coasting.
- Sources: [Agile42](https://www.agile42.com/en/agile-teams/scrum-lego-city), [Eficode GitHub](https://github.com/eficode-academy/lego-scrum-game)

## 2. PO Behavior Guide (Sprint-by-Sprint)

### Pre-Sprint: Vision Pitch
- Present a compelling, emotional vision: "Imagine a vibrant art school campus where creativity thrives — students painting in the courtyard, visitors exploring the gallery, performers on the amphitheater stage..."
- Be deliberately vague about specifics. Do NOT hand out acceptance criteria.
- When asked questions, answer briefly and only what was asked. Do not volunteer extra information.

### Sprint 1 Review: "The Harsh PO"
**Goal:** Create productive failure that drives learning.

**Behaviors:**
- Reject most items (aim for 60-80% rejection rate)
- Use aesthetic/functional critiques: "Where are the windows?" "This isn't symmetrical." "I appreciate color consistency — this is a mess of colors." "This doesn't feel like a campus building."
- If a team built something impressive but not aligned with your unstated criteria, still reject it. Do not let them convince you.
- Do NOT be mean or personal — be disappointed and specific. Frame as: "This isn't what I envisioned" not "This is bad."
- Key phrase: "Did you ask me what I wanted?"

**What creates the best "aha moments":**
- Rejecting high-effort work because teams assumed instead of asked
- Pointing out that no one came to ask questions during the build
- Showing that individual team members built in isolation without coordinating

### Sprint 2 Review: "The Coaching PO"
**Goal:** Reward improvement while maintaining standards.

**Behaviors:**
- Accept work that meets criteria (teams should be asking now)
- Be encouraging about improvements: "I can see you asked about symmetry — this is much better"
- Still reject items that don't meet standards, but explain why more clearly
- Optional power move: Leave the room for 2-3 minutes during the build to simulate PO unavailability. This teaches teams to front-load their questions during planning.
- Introduce or hint at Definition of Done if teams haven't developed one organically

### Sprint 3 Review: "The Celebrating PO"
**Goal:** Demonstrate the improvement arc that Scrum creates.

**Behaviors:**
- Be genuinely enthusiastic about quality improvements
- Accept most work (teams should be delivering well by now)
- Point out the contrast: "Look at how different this is from Sprint 1"
- If teams are coasting, introduce a surprise priority change to test adaptability
- Celebrate the campus as a whole — have teams physically assemble their pieces together

## 3. Definition of Done Recommendations

### What Experienced Trainers Recommend

**Don't introduce DoD before Sprint 1.** Let its absence be felt. When teams get work rejected in Sprint 1 Review, the pain creates the teachable moment for why DoD matters.

**Best approach for our workshop (3 options, in order of preference):**

1. **Let it emerge from the Retro (Recommended):** After Sprint 1 rejection, during the retrospective, ask: "How would you know if something is 'done' before showing it to me?" Let teams create their own DoD. This is the most powerful learning.

2. **Introduce after Sprint 1 Review:** If teams don't naturally arrive at it, provide a simple DoD after Sprint 1: "Done means: (a) it meets the PO's stated preferences, (b) it integrates with the campus layout, (c) team agrees it's complete."

3. **Provide upfront (least recommended):** Only do this if time is very tight. Loses the learning moment.

**Practical DoD criteria that work well for Lego:**
- Meets PO's stated acceptance criteria (once teams have asked for them)
- Consistent scale with other buildings
- Includes functional details (doors, windows, paths as applicable)
- Physically integrates into the campus layout
- Team collectively agrees it's complete (not just one person's work)

## 4. Best Debrief Questions

### Tier 1: Questions That Create Deepest Learning

These are the questions experienced trainers say produce the most powerful insights:

1. **"How did it feel when I rejected your work in Sprint 1?"**
   - Opens emotional processing. Connects to real-world stakeholder feedback.
   - Follow-up: "Has that ever happened in your real work? How did you handle it?"

2. **"What changed between Sprint 1 and Sprint 3 — and WHY did it change?"**
   - Forces articulation of the improvement arc. Teams usually identify: better communication, asking questions, planning more carefully, coordinating.
   - Follow-up: "What caused those changes? Was it the process or the people?"

3. **"When did you first go talk to me (the PO) during a sprint instead of waiting for review?"**
   - Highlights the communication lesson. If they didn't, ask: "Why not? What stopped you?"
   - Connect to real work: "How often do you check in with stakeholders mid-project?"

4. **"What assumptions did you make in Sprint 1 that turned out to be wrong?"**
   - Surfaces the hidden-criteria lesson. People assume they know what's wanted.
   - Connect: "Where do assumptions cause problems in your real work?"

### Tier 2: Deepening the Connection to Real Work

5. **"Did anyone feel frustrated? What did you do with that frustration?"**
   - Validates emotion. Connects to Scrum value of Courage.

6. **"How accurate were your estimates? What would help you estimate better?"**
   - Introduces concept of velocity and experience-based estimation.

7. **"If you could do the whole thing again, what would you do differently in the first 5 minutes?"**
   - Almost always: "Ask more questions" and "Plan before building."

8. **"Who emerged as a leader? Was that planned or did it happen naturally?"**
   - Connects to self-organizing teams.

### Tier 3: Making it Stick

9. **"What one Scrum practice from today could you use in your work next week?"**
   - Forces concrete application. Write answers on sticky notes.

10. **"Which Scrum value did you experience most during the Lego simulation?"**
    - (Already in our slides — good! Have them write it on sticky notes with a specific example.)

## 5. Common Pitfalls and Fixes

### Pitfall 1: Everyone Builds Alone
**What happens:** Each person grabs a backlog item and builds independently. No coordination.
**Why it matters:** This is actually desirable in Sprint 1 — it's a natural anti-pattern that Scrum corrects.
**Fix:** Don't intervene. Let Sprint 1 Review expose the problem. During retro, ask: "How many of you were working on the same campus but never talked to each other?"

### Pitfall 2: PO Is Too Nice in Sprint 1
**What happens:** Facilitator feels bad rejecting work, especially from non-technical people who are trying hard.
**Fix:** Commit to the role before starting. Remind yourself: being harsh now creates the best learning. You can debrief the emotional impact later. Frame rejections as disappointment, not criticism.

### Pitfall 3: Teams Get Genuinely Frustrated or Shut Down
**What happens:** Some participants take rejection personally, especially in non-technical groups unfamiliar with iterative feedback.
**Fix:**
- After Sprint 1 Review, immediately transition to Retro with empathy: "I know that felt rough. That's intentional. Let's talk about why."
- Acknowledge effort explicitly: "You built a LOT. The issue wasn't effort — it was alignment."
- If someone is visibly upset, break character briefly: "As your trainer, not your PO: this frustration is the exact lesson. The process is designed to fix this."

### Pitfall 4: Teams Get Competitive Instead of Collaborative
**What happens:** With 2 teams of 3-4, they compete instead of building one campus together.
**Fix:** Emphasize in the vision pitch that they're building ONE campus together. During Sprint 2 planning, ask: "How do your buildings connect to the other team's buildings?" In debrief, discuss inter-team collaboration as analogous to cross-team dependencies.

### Pitfall 5: Retros Produce Vague "We should communicate more" Actions
**What happens:** Teams don't know how to be specific in retros.
**Fix:** Push for specifics: "What exactly will you do differently in the next 7 minutes? Give me one concrete action." Model an example: "Instead of 'communicate more,' try 'show the PO our work at the 4-minute mark.'"

### Pitfall 6: Sprint 1 Planning Takes Too Long
**What happens:** Teams spend 10+ minutes planning because they don't know what to do.
**Fix:** Timebox strictly at 5 minutes. Say: "You have 5 minutes to pick items and plan. Go." If they're confused, that's fine — confusion in Sprint 1 planning is part of the learning.

### Pitfall 7: Teams Finish Too Fast
**What happens:** A team completes their Sprint Backlog items with time left.
**Fix:** They can pull more items from the Product Backlog (teach pull-based flow). Or they can refine/improve existing work to meet higher quality standards. This rarely happens in Sprint 1 but might in Sprint 3.

### Pitfall 8: Lego Pieces Run Out or Are Insufficient
**What happens:** Creative participants exhaust the supply of specific pieces.
**Fix:** Have extra small detail pieces ready. Allow paper drawing as a supplement for items like signs, paths, or landscaping. Our backlog already notes "can be drawn" for some items — good.

## 6. Specific Recommendations for Our Workshop

Based on the research, here are concrete changes and additions tailored to our 6-8 non-technical art education staff workshop:

### A. Setup Changes

**Table Layout:**
- With 6-8 people split into 2 teams of 3-4, use two adjacent tables with a shared "campus assembly" area between them (a third table or floor space). This physically reinforces that they're building one campus, not two separate projects.
- Put the Scrum board on the wall directly visible from both tables.
- Place Lego in a SHARED central pile between the tables, not divided per team. This creates natural resource negotiation (mirrors real-world constraints).

**Timer:**
- Use a large visible timer (phone on a stand, or project on a screen). This is emphasized by every source and we don't currently mention it in the facilitator guide.

### B. Facilitator Guide Additions

**Add to Sprint 1 instructions:**
- "Do NOT share acceptance criteria proactively. Keep these hidden: (1) I like symmetry, (2) Buildings need windows and doors, (3) Colors should be consistent per building, (4) Scale should be consistent across buildings, (5) Buildings need to connect to pathways. Only reveal criteria if teams ASK specific questions."
- "If teams ask 'What do you want?', respond vaguely: 'A beautiful art school campus. Impress me.' Force them to ask specific questions."

**Add to Sprint 2 instructions:**
- "Consider leaving the room for 2-3 minutes during the build to simulate PO unavailability. This teaches teams to front-load questions during planning."

**Add to Sprint 3 instructions:**
- "If teams are performing well, introduce a surprise: 'I just learned we're hosting a visiting artist next week — we need an outdoor sculpture installation. It's now the top priority.' This tests adaptability."

### C. Definition of Done Timing

**Current state:** Our slides mention DoD in the theory section (Slide 20) but don't explicitly connect it to the Lego exercise.

**Recommendation:** After Sprint 1 Review rejection, during the retrospective, ask: "How would you know if something is 'done' before showing it to me?" Let them create their own DoD on a sticky note and post it on the Scrum board. This is the most powerful approach per multiple sources. Reference back to Slide 20 concepts during the debrief.

### D. Debrief Enhancements

**Current debrief questions (4 questions, 15 min):**
Our current questions are solid but can be strengthened. Consider this sequence:

1. Keep: "What patterns emerged across the three Sprints?"
2. Keep: "How did it feel when I rejected your work?" — add follow-up: "Has that ever happened in your real work?"
3. Add: "When did you first go talk to me during a sprint instead of waiting?" (or "Why didn't you?")
4. Add: "What assumptions did you make in Sprint 1 that turned out wrong?"
5. Keep: "How does this connect to your real work?" — make more specific: "What one practice from today could you try in your work next week?"
6. Add (for art education context): "Think about how your students feel when they get feedback on their art. How is that similar to what you experienced today?"

The last question is particularly powerful for this audience — art educators give creative feedback daily and will immediately connect the PO-rejection experience to their own teaching practice.

### E. Art Education-Specific Framing

**For the vision pitch:** Lean into the art school theme heavily. This audience will connect with aesthetic and creative language. Instead of "build a campus," try: "Imagine you're designing the campus of your dreams — a place where every building inspires creativity. The gallery draws visitors from across the city. The amphitheater hosts performances under the stars. The cafe is where students sketch and debate. Make me fall in love with this campus."

**For the debrief:** Draw explicit parallels between:
- PO feedback on Lego = critique sessions with art students
- Iterative improvement across sprints = artistic revision process
- Definition of Done = assessment rubrics they use with students
- Sprint planning = lesson planning and curriculum design
- Backlog prioritization = deciding which projects to assign first

### F. Timing Adjustments

Our current timing is solid and aligns with what most sources recommend. One adjustment to consider:

- **Sprint 1 Review: 10 min (current) — keep this.** You need time to be thorough with rejections and let the emotional impact land.
- **Sprint 2-3 Review: 8 min (current) — fine.** Reviews get faster as quality improves.
- **Sprint 1 Retro: 8 min (current) — keep this.** The first retro needs more time because teams have the most to process.
- **Debrief: 15 min (current) — consider extending to 20 min if schedule allows.** Every source says "do not rush the debrief." With the additional questions above, 20 min would be better.

### G. Velocity Tracking (New Addition)

Add a simple velocity chart on the wall next to the Scrum board:
- After each Sprint Review, write the number of story points accepted
- Example: Sprint 1: 3 pts | Sprint 2: 8 pts | Sprint 3: 12 pts
- This creates a powerful visual of the improvement arc
- Reference during debrief: "Look at how your velocity changed. What caused that?"
