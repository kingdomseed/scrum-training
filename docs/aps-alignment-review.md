# APS Alignment Review

How this workshop compares to the Applying Professional Scrum (APS) class that inspired it — what aligns, what diverges, and what the options are.

**Source APS class:** December 2022, online via Mural, facilitated by John (licensed PST), ~137 teaching slides, 4 Sprints of 65 min each, audience was educators.

**This workshop:** March 18–19 2026, in-person, facilitated by Jason (PSM), ~83 slides, 3 Lego Sprints + 2 Product Sprints, audience is ArtWod startup team.

---

## Format Differences

| | APS (Source) | This Workshop |
|---|---|---|
| Delivery | Online (Mural) | In-person (physical) |
| Schedule | 9:00–5:00 (16 hrs) | 8:00–4:00 (16 hrs) |
| Collaboration tool | Mural boards | Sticky notes, wall space, Lego |
| Sprint simulation | 4 Sprints × 65 min (Mural-based) | Day 1: 3 Lego Sprints × ~25 min; Day 2: 2 Product Sprints × ~40 min |
| Sprint product | Mural-based presentations | Day 1: Lego campus; Day 2: open format (spec/pitch/prototype) |
| Team size | 4 teams of ~4–6 | Smaller group — likely 2–3 teams of 3–4 |
| Day 2 product | "Presentation about Scrum for 7th/8th graders" | "Next great feature release" for their own product |

### Covered but Adapted Significantly

| APS Topic | What Changed | Alignment Options |
|---|---|---|
| **Sprint simulation (4 × 65 min)** | Replaced with 3 Lego Sprints (~25 min each) on Day 1 and 2 Product Sprints (~40 min each) on Day 2. APS used Mural-based product building across all 4 Sprints with the same teams. | The APS approach used longer Sprints to give teams time to go deeper, and 4 Sprints gave an extra iteration cycle. A 4th Lego Sprint could be added by compressing other content, or the Product Sprints could be lengthened. However, 3 Lego Sprints is the Lego4Scrum standard and creates a clear three-act arc (chaos → coaching → celebration). The tradeoff is that a 4th Sprint lets teams experience sustained improvement. |
| **Sprint timing** | APS: Planning 10 min, Build 30 min, Review 15 min, Retro 10 min. Workshop Lego: Planning 3–5 min, Build 7 min, Review 5–10 min, Retro 5 min. | The Lego4Scrum timings follow the published Lego4Scrum book. Shorter Sprints keep energy high in person but reduce the depth of planning and review conversations. The APS timings allowed teams to have real discussions during Planning and produce substantial work during Build. The Product Sprints on Day 2 partially compensate with 20-min Create phases. |
| **Day 2 product ("Presentation about Scrum")** | APS had teams build a presentation explaining Scrum to 7th/8th graders. Workshop has teams build "something to present to stakeholders" about their own product in open format. | The APS approach forced participants to teach Scrum back, which reinforced learning through explanation. The workshop approach connects directly to participants' real work, which is more immediately applicable. Both serve different purposes: learning reinforcement vs. practical application. The "teach-back" approach is a known pedagogical technique (the Feynman method). |
| **Estimation technique** | APS covered both expert estimation and relative estimation with story points / planning poker. Workshop uses only T-shirt sizes (XS–XL). | T-shirt sizes are more accessible for non-technical teams and reduce the "precision trap" where teams debate whether something is a 3 or a 5. Planning poker could be introduced as a quick aside or as an optional mention. The APS treatment of multiple estimation approaches gave participants a broader toolkit. |
| **Team Start-up exercise** | APS had teams pick a mascot animal, share "3 things you want to learn," and discuss "added value of working as teams." Workshop uses introductions + Great Team exercise. | The workshop's approach covers similar ground. The mascot/team identity exercise creates team bonding, which matters more in multi-day workshops with strangers. For an intact team at ArtWod who already know each other, the current approach may be more appropriate. |
| **Retrospective depth** | APS dedicated specific slides to "Retrospective Force" and "Retrospective Commitments" — emphasizing retros as the primary improvement engine. Workshop covers retros as part of the events overview. | The APS framing treated the retrospective as the most important event for continuous improvement. Adding a brief section on "why retros matter more than you think" between the Lego debrief and Scrum Values could reinforce this without adding much time. |

### APS Topics Not Included

| APS Topic | What It Covered | Why It Might Matter | Alignment Options |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Incremental Product Development** | How products grow through increments over time; the difference between incremental and iterative. Included "Scrum Delivers Frequently" framing. | Helps participants understand why small batches and frequent delivery work better than big-bang releases. This is a foundational "why" concept. | Could be a 2–3 minute addition during Fundamentals — one slide showing incremental vs. waterfall delivery with a concrete example from ArtWod's context. |
| **Benefits of Scrum** | Explicit slide(s) on the measurable benefits: faster time to market, higher quality, happier teams, more predictability. | Useful for buy-in, especially with a startup audience where the CEO is invested. Gives participants language to articulate "why Scrum" to themselves and others. | Could be folded into the "Should We Use Scrum?" discussion on Day 2, or added as a brief slide in Fundamentals. |
| **Virginia Satir Change Model** | How teams move through change: Status Quo → Foreign Element → Chaos → Transforming Idea → Practice & Integration → New Status Quo. | Normalizes the discomfort of adopting new ways of working. Directly maps to the Lego simulation experience (Sprint 1 chaos is the "foreign element"). Particularly relevant for a startup about to adopt Scrum. | Could be referenced during the Lego debrief as a framing device: "What you just experienced follows a known pattern." Takes 3–5 minutes. |
| **12 Agile Principles exercise** | Teams mapped the 12 principles behind the Agile Manifesto to real situations. | The workshop covers the 4 Manifesto values but not the 12 principles. The principles add depth and specificity — "Deliver working software frequently" is actionable in a way that "Working Product over Documentation" is not. | Could be a handout reference rather than an exercise, or a brief "here are the 12 principles behind the values" slide. The full APS exercise (mapping principles to situations) takes 15–20 minutes. |
| **Collaboration & Cross-functionality** | Dedicated section on how Scrum Teams should have all skills needed. Included "T-shaped people" concept — deep in one area, broad across others. | T-shaped thinking helps participants understand why cross-functional teams outperform siloed specialists. For a small startup team where people likely already wear multiple hats, this could validate their existing behavior. | Could be a 1–2 minute addition to the Self-Management slide, or a brief mention during the Lego debrief when discussing how teams coordinated. |
| **Impediments** | What they are, why they matter, who removes them (Scrum Master). | The workshop covers the SM role but doesn't dedicate specific attention to impediment identification and removal as a practice. | Could be worked into the "Getting Started" section: "What are the biggest impediments you face today? Who would remove them on a Scrum Team?" |
| **Scaling (Nexus)** | Brief mention of how Scrum scales beyond one team. | Less relevant for a small startup team. However, if ArtWod is growing, understanding that Scrum can scale may be useful later. | Likely appropriate to skip for this audience. Could mention in passing during "Getting Started" if the team asks about scaling. |
| **"Take Away" summary slides** | End-of-section recap slides summarizing key concepts. | Provides memory anchors and signals transitions. Participants can photograph these as reference. | Adding brief recap bullets at section transitions is low-effort and could improve retention. The current section dividers use quotes; adding 3–4 key takeaway bullets to each would serve a similar purpose. |
| **"This is NOT proper Scrum" warning slides** | Called out common anti-patterns: Scrum-but, partial adoption, SM as project manager, etc. | Helps participants recognize dysfunction early. For a team just starting with Scrum, knowing what "wrong" looks like is as valuable as knowing what "right" looks like. | Could be a 5-minute addition during "Getting Started" on Day 2. Alternatively, a handout listing common anti-patterns could serve as a post-workshop reference. |
| **Pocket Principles** | Condensed reference card of Scrum principles for quick review. | Useful as a physical takeaway participants can pin to their desk. | Could be added to the handouts as a one-page reference card. |
| **Fact/Myth exercise** | Teams evaluated Scrum statements as fact or myth (e.g., "The Scrum Master manages the team"). | Interactive exercise that surfaces and corrects misconceptions. Especially useful after participants have some Scrum knowledge (Day 2). | Could replace or supplement the "Should We Use Scrum?" discussion. Takes 10–15 minutes. Alternatively, a few fact/myth questions could be woven into existing discussion prompts. |
| **Business Value / Measurement** | How to measure whether Scrum is delivering value. | Practical for stakeholders and POs. Helps the team articulate outcomes, not just outputs. | Could be a brief mention during the Planning with Scrum section, or a discussion prompt during the Product Sprint debrief. |
| **Organizational Transformation exercise** | Broader than "Should We Use Scrum?" — addressed how to introduce Scrum into an organization, deal with resistance, and support change. | For a startup adopting Scrum for the first time, the "how to start" question is directly relevant. | The current "Should We Use Scrum?" canvas partially covers this. The APS version went deeper into organizational readiness and change management. Could add 1–2 discussion prompts about anticipated resistance or organizational challenges. |

## Structural Comparison

### Slide Sequencing

The workshop follows the APS sequence closely:

```
APS Order                          Workshop Order
─────────                          ──────────────
1. Intro / Team Startup            1. Kickoff (Intro, Agreements, Great Team)
2. Fundamentals of Scrum           2. Fundamentals (Scrum def, Manifesto,
   (Manifesto, Complexity,            Stacey, Cynefin, Empiricism)
    Empiricism)
3. Exploring the Framework         3. The Framework (Roles, Events,
   (Roles, Events, Artifacts)         Artifacts, DoD)
4. Sprint Simulation (4 Sprints)   4. Lego Simulation (3 Sprints)
5. Scrum Values                    5. Scrum Values
6. Day 1 Close                     6. Day 1 Close
─── Day Break ───                  ─── Day Break ───
7. Deeper into Framework           7. Day 2 Opening + Deeper Dive
   (Events deep dive,                (Sprint flow, Self-Management)
    Self-Management)
8. Planning with Scrum             8. Planning with Scrum
   (Estimation, Refinement,          (Estimation, Refinement,
    Ready, Monitoring)                Ready, Monitoring)
9. Sprint Simulation contd.        9. Product Sprint (2 Sprints)
   (Sprints 3-4)
10. Getting Started                10. Getting Started
    (Should we use Scrum?,            (Should we use Scrum?,
     Org transformation)               Identify team)
11. Closing                        11. Closing
    (Appreciation, Reading)            (Appreciation, Reading)
```

The ordering is nearly identical. The main structural difference is that APS spread its 4 simulation Sprints across both days (2 on Day 1, 2 on Day 2 after Planning with Scrum content), while this workshop front-loads all Lego Sprints on Day 1 and uses Day 2 for the Product Sprint. The APS approach meant teams could apply Day 2 planning concepts (estimation, refinement, DoD) in their later Sprints. In this workshop, those concepts are taught between the two simulation types.

## Summary

**What aligns well:** The conceptual backbone — Fundamentals, Framework, Values, Planning — follows the APS sequence and covers the same core Scrum Guide content. The exercises (Great Team, Working Agreements, Feedback Wall, Appreciation Wall, Should We Use Scrum?) are all adapted from the APS originals.

**Where the workshop goes its own way:** The simulation approach (Lego + Product Sprint vs. 4 unified Mural Sprints), the estimation technique (T-shirt sizes vs. story points + planning poker), and the Day 2 product (real product focus vs. teach-back). Several APS topics are omitted entirely: Incremental Product Development, Virginia Satir, T-shaped people, 12 Principles, anti-patterns, and Fact/Myth.

**The tradeoff pattern:** The workshop consistently trades breadth for depth-of-experience. Where APS covered more topics with shorter exercises, this workshop covers fewer topics but gives participants more time in the simulation. For a startup team that needs to start doing Scrum (not just learn about it), this tradeoff may serve the goal well — they'll remember how it felt to work in Sprints more than they'd remember a slide about T-shaped people.
