### 1. Framework Block is 8 Consecutive Concept Slides (S12-S20)
**Problem:** Slides 12-20 (Framework Overview through Definition of Done) are 8 back-to-back lecture slides with zero interaction. This is the longest unbroken concept stretch in the entire deck and risks losing the audience mid-morning.

**Recommendation:** Insert a 5-minute interactive break after Slide 16 (Scrum Master). Options:

- A simple show-of-hands poll: "Which accountability do you think is hardest? Why?"
- A "Fact or Myth" quiz (3-4 statements about Scrum roles)

### 2. Exercise Slides Missing Visible Time Allocations
**Problem:** Slides 4, 27, 28, 32, 33 show exercises but don't display timing on-screen. The facilitator guide has timings, but participants benefit from seeing "You have 10 minutes" on the projected slide.

**Recommendation:** Add a visible time indicator (e.g., a small clock icon or bold text) to each exercise slide showing the allocated duration.

### 3. "10% of Sprint Capacity" for Refinement is Outdated
**Problem:** The 2020 Scrum Guide removed the specific "10% of Sprint capacity" guidance for Product Backlog Refinement. Our slide 42 may reference this.

**Recommendation:** Check slide 42 and change any "10%" reference to something like "as needed" or "the Scrum Team decides how and when refinement is done."

### 4. Schedule Discrepancy: 9-5 vs 9-3:30
**Problem:** The design doc says 9:00-5:00, but the facilitator guide says 9:00-3:30. These need to be consistent.

**Recommendation:** Confirm with Jason which schedule is correct and align both documents.

### 5. Lego Facilitation: PO Behavior Script Needed
**Problem:** The facilitator guide tells Jason to "play Product Owner" but doesn't script the critical Sprint 1 rejection behavior that makes Lego4Scrum effective.

**Recommendation:** Add a "PO Behavior Cheat Sheet" to the facilitator guide (full version in `docs/plans/lego4scrum-best-practices.md` Section 2):
- **Explicitly separate trainer hat from PO hat** — tell participants when you switch roles
- **Keep acceptance criteria HIDDEN** — don't volunteer them. Hidden criteria: symmetry, windows/doors, color consistency per building, consistent scale, buildings connect to pathways. Only reveal if teams ASK specific questions. If they ask "what do you want?", respond vaguely: "A beautiful art school campus. Impress me."
- **Sprint 1 Review (harsh PO):** Reject 60-80% of items. Key phrase: "Did you ask me what I wanted?" Point out nobody came to ask questions during the build.
- **Sprint 2 Review (coaching PO):** Accept improved work. Leave the room for 2-3 min during the build to simulate PO unavailability. Teach front-loading questions.
- **Sprint 3 Review (celebrating PO):** Accept most work. Point out the contrast with Sprint 1. If teams are coasting, inject a surprise priority change ("We need an outdoor sculpture installation — top priority").
- **Frame rejections as disappointment, not criticism:** "This isn't what I envisioned" not "This is bad."

### 6. Lego Definition of Done: Let It Emerge, Don't Prescribe
**Problem:** Slide 24 (Sprint Rules) covers sprint timing but doesn't address DoD for the Lego builds. Multiple experienced trainers say: **don't introduce DoD before Sprint 1** — let its absence be felt.

**Recommendation (revised based on research):** Do NOT add a DoD slide before the simulation. Instead:
- After Sprint 1 Review rejection, during the retrospective, ask: "How would you know if something is 'done' before showing it to me?"
- Let teams create their own DoD on a sticky note and post it on the Scrum board
- Reference back to Slide 20 (DoD theory) during the debrief
- Add this instruction to the facilitator guide under Sprint 1 Retro
- This is the #1 recommended approach across all sources — the pain of Sprint 1 creates the teachable moment

### 10. Lego Debrief Questions (S29) Could Be Stronger
**Problem:** Current debrief slide is good but misses some of the most powerful reflection questions from experienced trainers.

**Recommendation:** Ensure these questions are included (on slide or in facilitator notes):
1. "What happened in Sprint 1 vs Sprint 3? What changed?"
2. "When did you start asking the Product Owner questions? Why not earlier?"
3. "How accurate were your estimates? What would you do differently?"
4. "Did anyone feel like a Scrum Master was needed? What would they have done?"
5. "What would change if you couldn't talk to the PO during the sprint?"

### 11. Lego Sprint Timing Adjustment
**Problem:** Current Sprint Rules (S24) show Planning 5min → Build 7min → Review 5min → Retro 5min = 22min/sprint x 3 = 66min. The Lego4Scrum book recommends Planning 3min → Build 7min → Review 5min = 15min/sprint (no separate retro between sprints, just a brief "how can we improve?" question).

**Recommendation:** Consider shortening Planning to 3 minutes (forces urgency, more realistic) and making retro a quick 2-minute standup question rather than a seated exercise. Total becomes ~17min/sprint x 3 = 51min, leaving more time for the debrief which is where the real learning happens.

## Lego4Scrum Best Practices from Experienced Trainers

### Preparation Checklist
- [ ] Pre-make backlog cards on large sticky notes (don't improvise)
- [ ] Have extra LEGO pieces available (teams want decorative details)
- [ ] Set up physical Kanban board on wall BEFORE participants arrive
- [ ] Test the countdown timer on the projector
- [ ] Prepare a "penalty box" area for rejected items
- [ ] Have the DoD written on a visible poster/whiteboard

### Sprint 1 "Hard Landing" Strategy
The most experienced Lego4Scrum facilitators deliberately make Sprint 1 a learning failure:
- Teams typically work in isolation (everyone builds their own thing)
- At Review, PO rejects most items for inconsistency, scale, or missing the vision
- This creates the emotional catalyst for self-organization in Sprint 2
- **Critical:** Don't soften Sprint 1 feedback. The contrast between Sprint 1 and Sprint 3 IS the learning.

### PO Availability Pattern
- **Sprint 1:** Available for questions but don't volunteer information
- **Sprint 2:** Briefly leave the room (2-3 minutes) during the build phase
- **Sprint 3:** Be fully available and encouraging
- This progression teaches teams about stakeholder management and proactive communication

### Common Facilitator Mistakes
1. **Being too nice in Sprint 1** — undermines the entire learning arc
2. **Unclear acceptance criteria** — rejections feel arbitrary instead of educational
3. **Not respecting timeboxes** — extending builds "just 2 more minutes" teaches the wrong lesson
4. **Skipping the debrief** — the debrief is where 70% of the learning happens; never rush it
5. **Insufficient materials** — not having enough LEGO pieces limits creativity and causes frustration

### Debrief Facilitation Tips
- Use a circle format (everyone facing each other, not the screen)
- Go around the room so everyone speaks at least once
- Ask "What did you OBSERVE?" before "What did you LEARN?"
- Connect observations to real-world Scrum: "This is exactly what happens when..."
- Budget 15-20 minutes minimum; 30 minutes ideal

### Art Education-Specific Framing (for this audience)
Draw explicit parallels during the debrief:
- PO feedback on Lego = **critique sessions** with art students
- Iterative improvement across sprints = **artistic revision** process
- Definition of Done = **assessment rubrics** they use with students
- Sprint planning = **lesson planning** and curriculum design
- Backlog prioritization = deciding which **projects to assign first**

**Killer debrief question for this audience:** "Think about how your students feel when they get feedback on their art. How is that similar to what you experienced today?"

### Physical Setup Tips for Small Groups
- Use a **shared central Lego pile** between tables (not divided per team) — creates natural resource negotiation
- Place a **shared campus assembly area** between teams — reinforces "one campus, not two projects"
- Put the Scrum board on the wall directly visible from both tables
- Use a large visible countdown timer (phone on stand or projected)
- Track **velocity visibly** on the wall: write accepted story points after each sprint review (e.g., 3 → 8 → 12). Powerful visual for debrief.

### Retro Quality Tip
Push for **actionable** retro items. "We should communicate more" is not an action. "We will show the PO our work halfway through the next build" is. Model an example if needed.
