# Scrum Training Timeline Research Findings

> **Note:** This is a historical planning document from the early research phase. The final implementation diverged in several areas — see inline annotations marked [IMPLEMENTED DIFFERENTLY].

## 1. Typical APS (Applying Professional Scrum) 2-Day Class Schedule

**Format:** 2 days, ~8 hours instruction per day (16 hours total). Scrum.org requires minimum 14 hours of instruction time.

**Typical structure (from multiple providers):**
- 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM each day [IMPLEMENTED DIFFERENTLY: Final schedule is 8:00 AM – 4:00 PM]
- 1 hour lunch, two 15-min breaks per day
- Net instruction: ~6.5 hours/day = ~13 hours total
- Ratio: roughly 30% lecture/slides, 70% exercises and discussion

**APS Day 1 typical topics (compiled from Scrum.org, Rebel Scrum, Advised Skills, Red Tangerine):**
- Agile fundamentals and Scrum theory (empiricism, complexity)
- Scrum Framework overview (roles, events, artifacts)
- Working in Scrum Teams (simulation exercises)
- Sprint simulation (multiple sprints using exercises)

**APS Day 2 typical topics:**
- Product Backlog management and refinement
- Sprint Planning deep dive
- Definition of Done
- Self-management and collaboration
- Scaling considerations
- Practice assessment / closing

**Key insight:** APS courses are designed for beginners with no prior Scrum experience -- the closest official Scrum.org course to the proposed training.

## 2. Lego4Scrum Timing Recommendations

**Standard sprint timing per the official Lego4Scrum framework (Alexey Krivitsky):**

| Event | Duration |
|---|---|
| Sprint Planning | 3 minutes |
| Sprint (building) | 7 minutes |
| Sprint Review | 5 minutes |
| Sprint Retrospective | 5 minutes |
| **Total per sprint** | **~20 minutes** |

**Full workshop timing (3 sprints):**
- Pre-game setup (chartering, backlog creation, estimation): ~60 minutes
- 3 sprints x 20 min each: ~60 minutes
- Transitions, facilitation pauses between sprints: ~15 minutes
- Post-game debrief: ~15 minutes
- **Total: ~2-3 hours**

**Source variations:**
- Official lego4scrum.com: "120 minutes" minimum, "plan for 3 hours to be safe"
- hacerlobien.net PDF: ~120 min for complete simulation
- Spiros Dimitropoulos experience report: 2 hours tight, recommends 3 hours
- Lego4Scrum author workshops: 6-8 hours when combined with 20% lecturing / 80% simulation
- Agile Parrot guide: 30 minutes for backlog estimation alone

**For non-technical beginners, recommended adjustments:**
- Extend Sprint Planning to 5 minutes (they need more time to understand the process)
- Keep Sprint (building) at 7 minutes (time pressure is intentional and pedagogical)
- Extend Sprint Review to 7-8 minutes (allow more discussion)
- Keep Retrospective at 5 minutes
- Add 5-minute facilitation/teaching pauses between sprints
- **Adjusted total per sprint: ~27-30 minutes** [IMPLEMENTED DIFFERENTLY: Sprints are ~20-30 min with 3-5 min Planning, 7 min Build, 5-10 min Review, 5-8 min Retro]
- **3 sprints adjusted: ~90 minutes + pre-game (75 min) + debrief (15 min) = ~3 hours**

## 3. Pacing for Non-Technical Audiences

**Research consensus from multiple sources:**
- Less than 5% of attendees implement what they learn from lecture-only training
- Recommended ratio: 20-30% theory, 70-80% exercises/simulation
- Non-technical audiences benefit from MORE time on exercises, not more theory
- Exercises should be 15 minutes or less each, with immediate application
- Brief energizing activities between theory blocks prevent disengagement
- Keep intro to Scrum to 20 minutes max if audience is unfamiliar (Dimitropoulos)
- Use a "questions parking lot" -- don't answer every question in the moment

**Key recommendations for art education company:**
- Use visual, tactile exercises (Lego is perfect for this audience)
- Frame everything in their domain language, not software terms
- Sprint simulations with real product context are more engaging than abstract exercises
- Allow more debrief/discussion time than a technical audience would need
- The Scrum Guide itself is only 13 pages -- don't over-teach theory

## 4. Is 3 Lego Sprints + 2-3 Presentation Sprints Realistic?

**Analysis:**

Available time budget (2 days, 9am-5pm, 1hr lunch each):
- Day 1: 7 hours = 420 minutes
- Day 2: 7 hours = 420 minutes
- Total: 840 minutes

**Day 1 time needs (proposed):**
- Kickoff + working agreements: 45-60 min
- Scrum fundamentals (theory): 45-60 min
- Framework overview (theory): 30-45 min
- Morning break: 15 min
- Lunch: 60 min
- Afternoon break: 15 min
- Lego4Scrum simulation (3 sprints): 150-180 min (with extended timing for beginners)
- Day 1 wrap-up: 20-30 min
- **Total needed: ~440-480 min vs 480 min available**

**Verdict on Day 1:** Tight but feasible. The Lego simulation can run 2.5-3 hours including setup and debrief. The theory sections must be kept lean (max 90 minutes total slides across the morning).

**Day 2 time needs (proposed):**
- Review & deeper dive: 45-60 min
- Planning with Scrum (estimation, refinement): 45-60 min
- Morning break: 15 min
- Lunch: 60 min
- Afternoon break: 15 min
- Presentation Sprint simulation (2-3 sprints): 120-180 min
- Getting Started canvas: 30-45 min
- Closing: 20-30 min
- **Total needed: ~410-480 min vs 480 min available**

**Verdict on Day 2:** 3 presentation sprints is ambitious but possible if sprints are kept short (20-25 min each). 2 sprints is more comfortable and allows deeper discussion. **Recommendation: Plan for 2 sprints, have a 3rd sprint as a stretch goal.**

**Overall verdict:** The proposed structure is realistic but leaves very little buffer. The main risk is the presentation sprints on Day 2 taking longer than planned because participants are working on their real product and will want to discuss/debate more.

## Key Design Decisions & Rationale

### Why 2 Presentation Sprints (not 3) [IMPLEMENTED DIFFERENTLY: Renamed to "Product Sprint" with open format — spec, pitch, prototype, or whatever serves the product]
- Presentation sprints need longer work periods (20-25 min vs 7 min for Lego) because real creative work cannot be meaningfully compressed to 7 minutes
- 2 sprints give enough iteration to demonstrate improvement without feeling rushed
- The final presentations serve as a de facto 3rd "Review"
- If Day 2 runs ahead of schedule, a 3rd sprint can be inserted between 2:30 and 2:45 (skip the break, run a quick 15-min sprint)

### Why Sprint Planning is longer for Sprint 1 on each simulation
- First-time planners need more guidance
- Subsequent sprints naturally speed up -- this itself is a learning moment about team improvement

### Why the Lego simulation spans lunch
- Setup and estimation happen before lunch (mental warm-up)
- Building happens after lunch (physical energy)
- Avoids a post-lunch theory slump

### Why presentation sprints are 20-25 minutes (not 7)
- Creative/knowledge work takes longer than physical building
- Participants need time to discuss, draft, and iterate on real content
- Too-short sprints for presentation work would feel artificial and frustrating

### Buffer time
- Each day has ~15-30 min of built-in buffer
- Non-technical audiences tend to have more questions and need more discussion time
- Buffers prevent the schedule from derailing if one section runs long

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Theory sections run long | Strict timeboxing. Use timer. Move extra content to handouts. |
| Lego simulation takes longer than planned | Keep sprint building at 7 min (strict). Reduce to 2 sprints if needed. |
| Presentation sprints generate too much discussion | Timebox reviews strictly. Use parking lot for deep questions. |
| Post-lunch energy dip | Physical Lego activity on Day 1. Active sprint work on Day 2. No slides after lunch. |
| Participants want to keep working on presentations | Frame it: "This is a simulation to learn the process, not to finish the product." |
| Trainer is not a licensed PST | Avoid claiming Scrum.org or Scrum Alliance certification. Call it "Scrum training" or "Scrum workshop," not APS/CSM. Do not offer PSM I exam attempts. |
