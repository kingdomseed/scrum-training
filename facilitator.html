DAY 1

Wednesday, March 18, 2026

8:00 – 8:55 — Kickoff

55 min

Talking Points

  • Welcome everyone; set an informal, energetic tone
  • Introductions round: each person shares name, role, and one creative project they’re proud of — from ArtWod or their own practice
  • Frame the two days: participatory, hands-on, safe space to experiment

Working Agreements (10 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Each person writes 1–2 ground rules on sticky notes (one idea per note)
  • Post on wall, cluster similar ones, read aloud
  • Group agrees to honor them for both days — this is a social contract
  • Pre-seeded examples on slide (“Phones on silent,” “Be present,” “No bad ideas,” “Have fun”) are prompts, not prescriptions — let the group own it

"Great Team" Exercise (15 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Prompt: “Think of an AMAZING team you were on — a collaborative art project, a product team, a band, a theater production — any group. What made it great?”
  • One characteristic per sticky note
  • Post and cluster into themes on the wall
  • Debrief: "Notice how many of these are about people and interactions, not processes"
Set the tone early — this is participatory, not a lecture. If you're talking for more than 3 minutes straight, pause and ask a question.
Transition: "Now that we know what makes great teams, let's look at a framework designed to help teams be great."

8:55 – 9:45 — Fundamentals

50 min

Talking Points

  • Origins of Scrum — The “rugby scrum” metaphor from Takeuchi & Nonaka (1986): the team moves the ball forward as a unit, no handoffs, no sequential phases. Visible DISCUSS question: “How is your team’s current way of working more like a relay race or more like a rugby scrum?”
  • What is Scrum? — Use the Scrum Guide definition: “a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems.” The slide also shows a visible DISCUSS question: “What makes a problem ‘complex’ vs. just ‘complicated’?”
  • Deliver Early, Learn Often — Quick slide contrasting traditional (plan-build-deliver once) with incremental (plan-build-deliver-learn-repeat). Key message: Scrum delivers working product frequently so you can learn and adapt before it’s too late. Ask: “Has your team ever built something for weeks, only to learn it wasn’t what was needed?”
  • Agile Manifesto values — Don’t just read them. The slide includes a visible DISCUSS question: “Which of these values resonates most with you today?” Note: the slide layout uses top/bottom (not left/right), so the subtitle reads “While there is value in the items below, we value the items above more.”
  • Stacey Matrix (S9) — The Stacey Matrix shows how complexity increases as requirements and technology become less certain. Point out the four zones: Simple, Complicated, Complex, Chaotic. Ask participants where their current work falls. Scrum is designed for the Complex zone — where more is unknown than known.
  • Cynefin framework (S10) — Cynefin (pronounced "kuh-NEV-in") provides a decision-making framework. Each domain has a different response pattern:
    • Complex: Probe → Sense → Respond (this is where Scrum operates)
    • Complicated: Sense → Analyze → Respond
    • Clear: Sense → Categorize → Respond
    • Chaotic: Act → Sense → Respond
    The key insight: in complex work, you can't analyze your way to the answer — you have to experiment.
    Discussion question (prompt verbally — not on slide): "How does each domain change the way you should make decisions?"
  • Empiricism — Three pillars: Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation. These are the foundation everything else rests on.
  • Empiricism table (S12) — Walk through the table row by row. For each Scrum event, show how it demonstrates all three pillars of empiricism: Transparency (making work visible), Inspection (examining progress), and Adaptation (adjusting the plan). This slide reinforces that empiricism isn’t abstract — it’s built into every Scrum event.
    Discussion question (visible on slide): “Which of these three pillars does your team practice today — and which one is missing?”

"What Is YOUR Product?" (3 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers

  • After the “What is a Product?” discussion slide, prompt participants to answer three questions on a sticky note: What value does your product deliver? Who are your users, customers, and stakeholders? What makes it worth working on?
  • Start a 3-minute timer
  • Invite a few volunteers to share with the room
  • Important: Have them keep the sticky note — it comes back on Day 2.
Keep this section interactive. Ask questions, don't lecture. Every concept should connect to something they already know.
“Plans are worthless, but planning is everything.” — Dwight D. Eisenhower. Use this quote when discussing why Scrum embraces change rather than fighting it.
Transition: "These principles need a structure. Let's look at how Scrum puts them into practice."
9:45 – 10:00 — Break (15 min)

10:00 – 10:40 — The Framework

40 min

Talking Points

  • Scrum Framework overview — Show the big picture diagram. Everything fits inside the Sprint container.
  • Three accountabilities:
    • Product Owner — maximizes value, owns the Product Backlog
    • Developers — create the Increment each Sprint
    • Scrum Master — serves the team, removes impediments, coaches Scrum
  • The Sprint — The Sprint is itself an event — a fixed-length container (1 month or less) for all other events. Everything happens inside Sprints.
  • Events overview — Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective
  • Artifacts & Commitments — Product Backlog (Product Goal), Sprint Backlog (Sprint Goal), Increment (Definition of Done)
  • Definition of Done — The shared quality standard. If it doesn’t meet the DoD, it’s not done. Visible DISCUSS question: “What does ‘Done’ look like for your team’s work right now?”

Match the Accountability (interactive slide)

  • The slide has a drag-and-drop exercise: participants match scenario cards to the correct Scrum accountability (PO, Developers, Scrum Master)
  • Let participants discuss and sort the cards before revealing the answers
  • Use the Check Answers button on the slide to reveal correct placements
Visible DISCUSS questions on the framework slides to use as prompts:
  • Product Owner: “Who in your organization is closest to this role today?”
  • Developers: “In Scrum, ‘Developers’ means anyone doing the work — not just coders. Who are the developers on your team?”
  • The Sprint: “Imagine your team had to ship a finished piece of course content every two weeks. What would you do differently?”
Connect each concept to their ArtWod context. Ask: “Who plays these roles in your work now?” and “What would a Sprint look like for your team?” The Scrum Master slide includes an ArtWod-specific prompt: “Think about the last time a project stalled at ArtWod. What was actually blocking progress — and whose job was it to remove that block?”
Transition: "You've heard the theory. Now let's experience it. Time to build something."

10:40 – 11:30 — Lego Pre-game

50 min

Talking Points

  • Transition from theory to practice: “You’ve learned the framework. Now you’re going to experience it.”
  • Quote: “In theory there is no difference between a theory and a practice, but in practice there is.”

Setting the Stage (~3 min)

  • Explain why Lego: they’re about to experience everything they just discussed
  • A PO will pitch a vision, they’ll form teams, discover what to build, and then build it in 3 Sprints

Form Teams + Select Scrum Masters (~5 min)

  • Self-organize into 2 teams
  • Each team selects their own Scrum Master
  • SM coaches and removes impediments — does NOT manage or assign work
  • Watch for: teams struggling to self-organize (this is itself a lesson)

Vision Pitch (~5 min)

  • PUT ON YOUR PO HAT. Say: “I’m now speaking as your Product Owner.”
  • Pitch your city vision LIVE — don’t read from slides
  • A good vision has: compelling purpose (WHY), users (WHO), needs (WHAT)
  • Stay deliberately vague on specifics. “Impress me.”
  • After pitching, ask: “How does it feel hearing this vision?”

Sprint Rules Overview (~2 min)

  • 3 Sprints: Planning (5 min), Build (8 min), Review (7 min), Retro (10 min)
  • Both teams build ONE city — they must coordinate
  • Only items accepted by the PO in Review count as Done

Materials Checklist for This Block

  • Sticky notes (lots, multiple colors), markers
  • Painter’s tape for wall
  • Large paper for Scrum boards (or use whiteboard)
Buffer time before lunch. Use it for questions or let teams settle in. Product discovery happens AFTER lunch — don’t start backlog creation now.
Transition: “Enjoy lunch. When you come back, we discover what to build — then we build it.”
11:30 – 12:30 — Lunch (60 min)
During lunch: Set up the product discovery wall (large paper or cleared wall space for sticky notes). Set up Sprint Backlog areas for each team (tape columns on wall or use Scrum board handout). Put out Lego boxes.

12:30 – 1:10 — Product Discovery

40 min

Talking Points

  • Frame this as discovery, not planning: “Before we build, we need to understand WHO we’re building for and WHAT they need.”
  • The wall reads left to right: Users → Needs → Features. Features become the Product Backlog.

Step 1: Who Are Our Users? (~5 min)

Materials: Large sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Ask: “Who will live in, visit, and work in this city?”
  • Identify 2–3 user types (personas)
  • Write each on a large sticky note on the LEFT side of the wall
  • Example personas: residents/creators, visitors/investors, staff

Step 2: What Do They Need? (~15 min)

Materials: Sticky notes (different color from Step 1), markers

  • For each user type: “What do they need? Imagine a day in their life.”
  • Write one need per sticky note, place in the CENTER of the wall next to the user
  • CRITICAL: If teams jump to features (“a house!” “a shop!”) — redirect: “That’s a building. What NEED does it serve?”
  • Needs are things like: a place to sleep, somewhere to work, a way to eat, transportation, recreation
  • 10 min writing + 5 min discussing/grouping

Step 3: What Do We Build? (~10 min)

Materials: Sticky notes (third color), markers

  • NOW turn needs into buildable features
  • Write one item per sticky note on the RIGHT side of the wall
  • The wall reads left to right: Users → Needs → Features
  • These features ARE the Product Backlog

Step 4: Estimate & Prioritize (~10 min)

  • Silent sorting: sort features by T-shirt size (XS, S, M, L, XL) without talking (2–3 min)
  • PO re-prioritizes by business value
  • Logic: infrastructure/landscape first, simple items first, complex items later
Use three different sticky note colors for the three layers (Users, Needs, Features) so the wall is visually clear.
Transition: “You know who you’re building for, what they need, and what to build. Time to sprint.”

1:10 – 2:10 — Lego Sprints 1 & 2

60 min

PO Behavior Cheat Sheet

Explicitly separate your trainer hat from your PO hat: “I’m now speaking as your Product Owner.”

Hidden acceptance criteria (do NOT volunteer — only reveal if teams ASK specific questions):

  • Symmetry in building layout
  • Windows and doors on every building
  • Color consistency per building
  • Consistent scale across buildings
  • Buildings connected by pathways

If asked “What do you want?”: “A beautiful city. Impress me.”

Frame rejections as disappointment, not criticism: “This isn’t what I envisioned” not “This is bad.”

Key PO questions during Review:

  • “What do you guys see here?”
  • “How do you like the product we are building?”
  • “Will you agree that this looks…?”
  • “What is missing and misaligned?”
  • “Imagine you are a user entering this building…”

Sprint 1 — Let Them Fail (~30 min)

  • Planning (5 min): Everyone to the board — teams pull items from Product Backlog into Sprint Backlogs. Coordinate across teams — one city. Confidence vote (fist of five). Watch for: teams not coordinating, SMs planning instead of devs, no one asking PO questions.
  • Build (8 min): Start timer. Leave room for 2–3 min (“business trip”). Note who builds alone vs coordinates, whether anyone asks PO questions (probably not). End at exactly 8 min: “Hands up!”
  • Review (7 min): “WHERE IS MY CITY?!” Demand integration — 95% of teams won’t have integrated. Inspect and Adapt, not Inspect and Accept. Be disappointed, not mean. “Did you ask me what I wanted?” Reject items that don’t meet hidden criteria.
  • Retro (10 min): Team retro 5 min + Overall retro 5 min. THIS IS WHERE DEFINITION OF DONE EMERGES. Ask: “How would you know if something is done BEFORE showing it to me?” Have teams create their Definition of Done on a sticky note. Push for actionable items: “Show PO work at 4-min mark” not “communicate more.”
Sprint 1 will be chaos. That’s the point. Do NOT soften your feedback. The contrast between Sprint 1 and Sprint 3 IS the learning.

Sprint 2 — Coach Them (~30 min)

  • Planning (5 min): Watch for teams referencing retro actions, using their Definition of Done, asking PO questions. Confidence vote.
  • Build (8 min): Be available but don’t volunteer criteria. Note improvements from Sprint 1.
  • Review (7 min): “WHERE IS MY CITY?!” Accept improved work that meets criteria. Still reject items that miss criteria — but explain why. Acknowledge progress. Update velocity tally on wall.
  • Retro (10 min): Team retro 5 min + Overall retro 5 min. Push for actionable items only. “Communicate more” is not an action. “What specific thing will you do differently in the next 8 minutes?”
Watch for improvements in communication, planning, and quality. Note these for the debrief.
2:10 – 2:25 — Break (15 min)

2:25 – 2:55 — Lego Sprint 3

30 min

Sprint 3 — Celebrate (~30 min)

  • Planning (5 min): Teams should be confident and realistic. Be fully available. If injecting surprise: “We’re hosting a visiting artist. Outdoor sculpture — top priority!”
  • Build (8 min): Stay present. If team finishes early, they can pull more items from Product Backlog (pull-based flow).
  • Review (7 min): “WHERE IS MY CITY?!” Accept most work. Have teams physically assemble pieces into one city. Celebrate the contrast with Sprint 1. Update velocity on wall — the improvement arc tells the story.
  • Retro (10 min): Team retro 5 min + Overall retro 5 min. Focus on the full journey: “Compare Sprint 1 vs Sprint 3. What changed?”

2:55 – 3:15 — Lego Debrief

20 min

Debrief (20 min)

Observe / Learn / Connect (5 min write + 15 min discuss)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers

Each person writes one sticky note per category:

  • Observe: “Write one thing you noticed change across the Sprints.”
  • Learn: “Write the biggest lesson from the simulation.”
  • Connect: “Write one way this connects to your real work.”

Post notes together, then debrief as a group.

Discussion Prompts

  • “What patterns emerged across the three Sprints?”
  • “When did you start asking the Product Owner questions? Why not earlier?”
  • “How did it feel when I rejected your work?” → connects to stakeholder feedback
  • “What assumptions did you make in Sprint 1 that turned out wrong?”
  • “If you could do it again, what would you do differently in the first 5 minutes?”
Virginia Satir Change Model (mention verbally, 2–3 min): What they experienced follows a known pattern — Status Quo → Foreign Element (Sprint 1 chaos) → Resistance → Transforming Idea (retro insights) → Practice → New Status Quo (Sprint 3 flow). “The discomfort you felt in Sprint 1 is normal. You already made it through in 90 minutes.”
This debrief is the most important part of the entire simulation. Do not rush it. Let the group make the connections themselves.
Transition: “Now let’s connect what you experienced to the five Scrum values.”

3:15 – 3:30 — Scrum Values

15 min

Talking Points

  • Five values: Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage
  • Don’t just list them — connect each value to the Lego experience
  • Verbal discussion question: “Which came naturally? Which was hardest?”
  • Keep this conversational — no sticky note exercise, just the Five Values slide with group discussion
Transition: “Let’s put some language around what we experienced, then close out the day.”

3:30 – 4:00 — Day 1 Closing

30 min

Talking Points

  • Terminology slide: Practices matter more than labels. The goal is better outcomes, not perfect vocabulary.

Feedback Wall (5–10 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Three columns: What surprised me today? | What do I want to learn more about? | How does this connect to our work?
  • Each person writes at least one sticky for each column
  • Review together as a group

Day 1 Closing

  • Preview tomorrow: deeper dive into planning, estimation, and a new hands-on exercise
  • Optional homework: Read the Scrum Guide (only 13 pages)
  • Review the Parking Lot — address any unanswered questions or flag for tomorrow
  • Thank everyone for their participation and energy
Take the Feedback Wall to your hotel room. Analyze patterns — what surprised them tells you what to reinforce on Day 2. What they want to learn more about tells you where to add depth.

DAY 2

Thursday, March 19, 2026

8:00 – 8:15 — Day 2 Opening

15 min

Talking Points

  • Open with: “What stuck with you overnight?” — Let people share freely
  • Pair exercise: turn to your neighbor and explain what Scrum is in 2 minutes. Then share one thing that surprised you and one thing you want to understand better today
Transition: “Let’s go deeper into the Sprint events you experienced yesterday.”

8:15 – 8:45 — Deeper Dive

30 min

Talking Points

  • Review the Sprint Events Flow: Planning → Daily Scrum → Sprint Review → Retrospective
  • Discuss self-management: “Scrum Teams are cross-functional and self-managing. What does that mean in practice?” The slide now includes a bullet on “T-shaped” people — deep in one area, broad enough to help across others. For a startup like ArtWod where people already wear multiple hats, validate that and name the pattern.
  • ArtWod-specific prompt: “Where at ArtWod do teams already self-manage? Where does someone else decide the who, what, or how?”
Transition: “Today we go deeper into the planning side of Scrum.”

8:45 – 9:30 — Planning with Scrum

45 min

Talking Points

  • Estimation: Relative sizing, not absolute. "Is this bigger or smaller than that?" T-shirt sizes (XS, S, M, L, XL) as an accessible technique.
  • Product Backlog Refinement: Ongoing activity, not an event. Break big items down, add detail, re-order. The Scrum Team decides how and when refinement is done — this is no longer prescribed as a specific percentage of Sprint capacity.
  • Definition of Ready: Clear, Sized, Testable. An item should meet these criteria before entering Sprint Planning.
  • Monitoring Sprint Progress: Burndown/burnup charts, transparency of work remaining.

Estimation Exercise (10 min)

Materials: Sticky notes or index cards with work items

  • Teams pick 5–8 real tasks from their current work
  • Estimate using T-shirt sizes (XS, S, M, L, XL)
  • Compare and discuss differences — the conversation matters more than the number
Emphasize that estimation is about shared understanding, not precision. "The conversation is the value, not the number."
Transition: "Time for a break, then we'll put this into practice with a new exercise."
9:30 – 9:45 — Break (15 min)

9:45 – 10:15 — Product Sprint Setup

30 min

"Remember This?" — Day 1 Callback

  • Have participants retrieve their "What Is YOUR Product?" sticky note from Day 1
  • Each person shares their sticky note, then split into two teams. Each team picks a product area to focus on.
  • This reconnects them to their product thinking and sets up the Product Sprint

Talking Points

  • The Challenge: In 2 Sprints, create something to present to stakeholders that moves your product forward. Format is open — spec, pitch, prototype, or whatever serves the product.
  • Teams pick their own PO this time; they own the process now
  • What to include: problem statement, value proposition, target audience, how it works (show, don’t just tell), next steps

Setup

Materials: Paper, markers, poster boards, phones for reference

  • Distribute materials to each team
  • Each team selects their own Product Owner
  • Teams define their own Definition of Done for the presentation
  • Remind them to use their Scrum boards
Transition: "You have 80 minutes for Sprint 1. Go."

10:15 – 11:15 — Product Sprint 1 & Debrief

60 min
  • Planning (5 min): Teams decide what they'll accomplish in this Sprint
  • Create (20 min): Teams work on their presentation
  • Review (10 min): Each team shows progress; other teams provide feedback too
  • Retro (5 min): Quick reflection on what to improve for Sprint 2

Remaining time is buffer for transitions and discussions.

This sprint is more open-ended than the Lego exercise. Let teams find their own rhythm. Observe how they apply what they learned yesterday.
Transition: "Lunch break. Sprint 2 starts right after."
11:15 – 12:30 — Lunch (75 min)

12:30 – 1:30 — Product Sprint 2

60 min
  • Planning (5 min): Incorporate feedback from Sprint 1 Review
  • Create (20 min): Polish and complete the presentation
  • Review (10 min): Final iteration feedback
  • Retro (5 min): Note: this retro uses different columns than the others — “What went well?” / “What improved?” / “What did you learn?” — to reflect on the full journey

Remaining time is buffer for transitions and final prep.

Transition: "Now it's showtime. Final presentations."
1:30 – 1:45 — Break (15 min)

1:45 – 2:30 — Final Presentations & Debrief

45 min

Talking Points

  • This IS the final Sprint Review — frame it that way
  • Each team gets 10–15 minutes to present
  • Encourage feedback from other teams after each presentation
  • Celebrate the work and the improvement from Sprint 1 to the final product
Make this celebratory. The point is not perfection — it's the iterative improvement and the team's growth through the process.
Transition: "You've now experienced Scrum firsthand. Let's talk about taking it home."

2:30 – 2:50 — Your Scrum Startup Kit

20 min

Talking Points

  • “The Bare Minimum to Start” checklist slide — Walk through the seven items: PO, SM, Developers, Product Backlog (first 5 items), Definition of Done, Sprint Length, First Sprint date. Frame as: “You experienced every one of these during the Lego simulation and Product Sprint. Now we’re filling them in for real.”
  • “About the Scrum Master” slide — Present three common approaches for teams just starting: internal part-time (team member wears SM hat), external coach (experienced SM helps team get started, then transitions internally), or dedicated hire. The Scrum Guide defines SM as an accountability, not optional — but how to fill it is a team decision. Let the team discuss what makes sense for ArtWod. No one on the team is currently certified; the role is most intensive early on and decreases as Scrum becomes habitual.
  • “Watch Out For” anti-patterns slide — Four common patterns that undermine Scrum: Scrum-But, SM as project manager, no real Increment, unresolved impediments. Keep this conversational (3–5 min).
Transition: “Now it’s your turn. Self-organize and fill in your Scrum Startup Kit.”

2:50 – 3:20 — Self-Organize

30 min

Fill In Your Scrum Startup Kit (30 min)

Materials: Handout 4 (Team Canvas), sticky notes, markers, poster paper

  • Teams use the handout canvas to commit to each item: PO, SM approach, Developers, first 5 backlog items, Definition of Done, Sprint length, first Sprint date
  • Step back and let them self-organize — this IS Scrum in action
  • Be available for questions but don’t drive the conversation
  • If stuck on SM, acknowledge the options discussed on the slide. No wrong answer.
  • 5-minute warning at 25 min: “Start wrapping up — you’ll share back with the group.”

3:20 – 3:40 — Share Back

20 min

Talking Points

  • Each team (or the full group as one team) presents their Scrum Startup Kit: roles, backlog items, DoD, Sprint length, start date
  • Ask clarifying questions: “When exactly is Sprint 1?” “Who’s the PO?” “What’s the first thing you’d build?”
  • If SM is unresolved, acknowledge it as an open item and discuss next steps
  • Celebrate the decisions — this is real, not hypothetical
Transition: “You have a plan. Let’s close out these two days together.”

3:40 – 4:00 — Appreciation & Closing

20 min

Appreciation Wall

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Each person writes an appreciation for someone else in the workshop
  • Post on the wall; read a few aloud
