DAY 1

Wednesday, March 11, 2026

8:00 – 8:55 — Kickoff

55 min

Talking Points

  • Welcome everyone; set an informal, energetic tone
  • Introductions round: each person shares name, role, and what they hope to get from the workshop
  • Frame the two days: participatory, hands-on, safe space to experiment

Working Agreements (10 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Each person writes 1–2 agreements on sticky notes (e.g., "phones on silent," "no wrong answers")
  • Post on wall, cluster similar ones together
  • Group agrees to honor them for both days

"Great Team" Exercise (15 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Prompt: "Think of a great team you've been on. What made it great?"
  • One characteristic per sticky note
  • Post and cluster into themes on the wall
  • Debrief: "Notice how many of these are about people and interactions, not processes"
Set the tone early — this is participatory, not a lecture. If you're talking for more than 3 minutes straight, pause and ask a question.
Transition: "Now that we know what makes great teams, let's look at a framework designed to help teams be great."

8:55 – 9:45 — Fundamentals

50 min

Talking Points

  • What is Scrum? — Use the Scrum Guide definition: "a lightweight framework that helps people, teams, and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems"
  • Agile Manifesto values — Don't just read them. Ask the group: "Which of these resonates with you? Which feels hardest?" Discuss, don't lecture. Note: the slide layout uses top/bottom (not left/right), so the subtitle reads "While there is value in the items below, we value the items above more."
  • Stacey Matrix (S9) — The Stacey Matrix shows how complexity increases as requirements and technology become less certain. Point out the four zones: Simple, Complicated, Complex, Chaos. Ask participants where their current work falls. Scrum is designed for the Complex zone — where more is unknown than known.
  • Cynefin framework (S10) — Cynefin (pronounced "kuh-NEV-in") provides a decision-making framework. Each domain has a different response pattern:
    • Complex: Probe → Sense → Respond (this is where Scrum operates)
    • Complicated: Sense → Analyze → Respond
    • Clear: Sense → Categorize → Respond
    • Chaotic: Act → Sense → Respond
    The key insight: in complex work, you can't analyze your way to the answer — you have to experiment.
    Discussion question (prompt verbally — not on slide): "How does each domain change the way you should make decisions?"
  • Empiricism — Three pillars: Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation. These are the foundation everything else rests on.
  • Empiricism table (S12) — Walk through the table row by row. For each Scrum event, show how it demonstrates all three pillars of empiricism: Transparency (making work visible), Inspection (examining progress), and Adaptation (adjusting the plan). This slide reinforces that empiricism isn't abstract — it's built into every Scrum event.
    Discussion question (prompt verbally): "Can you think of a time when skipping one of these pillars caused problems?"
Keep this section interactive. Ask questions, don't lecture. Every concept should connect to something they already know.
"Plans are worthless, but planning is everything." — Eisenhower. Use this quote when discussing why Scrum embraces change rather than fighting it.
Transition: "These principles need a structure. Let's look at how Scrum puts them into practice."
9:45 – 10:00 — Break (15 min)

10:00 – 10:40 — The Framework

40 min

Talking Points

  • Scrum Framework overview — Show the big picture diagram. Everything fits inside the Sprint container.
  • Three accountabilities:
    • Product Owner — maximizes value, owns the Product Backlog
    • Developers — create the Increment each Sprint
    • Scrum Master — serves the team, removes impediments, coaches Scrum
  • The Sprint — The Sprint is itself an event — a fixed-length container (1 month or less) for all other events. Everything happens inside Sprints.
  • Events overview — Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective
  • Artifacts & Commitments — Product Backlog (Product Goal), Sprint Backlog (Sprint Goal), Increment (Definition of Done)
  • Definition of Done — The shared quality standard. If it doesn't meet the DoD, it's not done.
Connect each concept to their context. Ask: "Who plays these roles in your work now?" and "What would a Sprint look like for your team?"
Transition: "You've heard the theory. Now let's experience it. Time to build something."

10:40 – 11:30 — Lego4Scrum Setup

50 min

Talking Points

  • Product Vision: "Art School Campus" — present it with enthusiasm but deliberate vagueness
  • Show the Product Backlog (12 items with relative sizes)
  • Explain Sprint cadence: Planning (3–5 min) → Build (7 min) → Review (5–10 min) → Retro (5–8 min)

Team Formation & Board Setup

Materials: Lego bricks, poster paper, painter's tape, markers

  • Teams self-organize into groups of 3–4
  • Each team sets up a physical Scrum board on the wall with 4 columns: Product Backlog | Sprint Backlog | Doing | Done
  • Distribute Lego bricks evenly across teams
  • Each team selects someone to facilitate their Daily Scrum (rotate each Sprint)
As PO, be deliberately vague at first. "I want a beautiful campus." Let teams ask questions. This teaches them the value of refinement and PO collaboration.
Transition: "Enjoy lunch. When you come back, we build."
11:30 – 12:30 — Lunch (60 min)

12:30 – 2:50 — Lego Sprints

140 min

🎭 PO Behavior Cheat Sheet

Explicitly separate your trainer hat from your PO hat. Tell participants when you switch roles: “I’m now speaking as your Product Owner.”

Hidden acceptance criteria (do NOT volunteer these — only reveal if teams ASK specific questions):

  • Symmetry in building layout
  • Windows and doors on every building
  • Color consistency per building
  • Consistent scale across buildings
  • Buildings connected by pathways

If asked “What do you want?” respond vaguely: “A beautiful art school campus. Impress me.”

Frame rejections as disappointment, not criticism: “This isn’t what I envisioned” not “This is bad.”

Sprint 1 — Harsh PO (~30 min)

  • Planning (5 min): Teams select backlog items and forecast how much they can build. They will overcommit — that's expected.
  • Build (7 min): Start the timer. Observe but don't intervene. Be available for questions but don't volunteer information.
  • Review (10 min): Reject 60–80% of items. Key phrase: “Did you ask me what I wanted?” Point out nobody came to ask questions during the build. “Where are the windows?” “This isn’t symmetrical.” “I didn’t ask for that.”
  • Retro (8 min): “What went well? What didn’t? What will you change?” Ask: “How would you know if something is ‘done’ before showing it to me?” — let teams create their own Definition of Done on a sticky note.
Sprint 1 will be chaos. That's the point. Do NOT soften your feedback. The contrast between Sprint 1 and Sprint 3 IS the learning.

Sprint 2 — Coaching PO (~30 min)

  • Planning (3 min): Should be faster now. Watch for teams adjusting their forecasts and asking better questions.
  • Build (7 min): Observe improvements in coordination. Leave the room for 2–3 minutes to simulate PO unavailability — teaches teams to front-load questions during Planning.
  • Review (8 min): Accept improved work that meets criteria. Be encouraging but maintain standards. Acknowledge progress.
  • Retro (5 min): Look for concrete changes from Sprint 1. Push for actionable items: “We will show the PO our work halfway through the build” not “We should communicate more.”
Watch for improvements in communication, planning, and quality. Call them out during the debrief — these are the patterns Scrum is designed to create.

Sprint 3 — Celebrating PO (~30 min)

  • Planning (3 min): Teams should be confident and realistic. Be fully available and encouraging.
  • Build (7 min): Expect better coordination and quality. If teams are coasting, inject a surprise priority change: “We need an outdoor sculpture installation — top priority!”
  • Review (8 min): Accept most work. Celebrate progress! Point out the contrast with Sprint 1: “Look how far you’ve come.”
  • Retro (5 min): Focus on the learning journey.

Debrief (15 min)

Discussion Prompts

  • “What patterns emerged across the three Sprints?”
  • “What happened in Sprint 1 vs Sprint 3? What changed?”
  • “When did you start asking the Product Owner questions? Why not earlier?”
  • “How did it feel when I rejected your work?” → connects to PO role and shared understanding
  • “How accurate were your estimates? What would you do differently?”
  • “Did anyone feel like a Scrum Master was needed? What would they have done?”
  • “Think about how your learners feel during their first art critique on the platform. How is that similar to what you experienced today?”
  • “How does this connect to your real work?”
This debrief is the most important part of the entire simulation. Do not rush it. Let the group make the connections themselves before you add anything.

2:50 – 3:20 — Scrum Values

30 min

Talking Points

  • Five values: Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage
  • Don't just list them — connect each value to the Lego experience
  • Ask: "Which value did you experience most during the simulation? Which was hardest?"

Feedback Wall

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Three columns: Surprises | Questions | Connections to My Work
  • Each person writes at least one sticky for each column
  • Review together as a group
Transition: "Let's wrap up Day 1 and preview what's coming tomorrow."

3:20 – 4:00 — Day 1 Closing

15 min

Talking Points

  • Preview tomorrow: deeper dive into planning, estimation, and a new hands-on exercise
  • Optional homework: Read the Scrum Guide (only 13 pages)
  • Review the Parking Lot — address any unanswered questions or flag for tomorrow
  • Thank everyone for their participation and energy

DAY 2

Thursday, March 12, 2026

8:00 – 8:45 — Day 2 Opening & Deeper Dive

45 min

Talking Points

  • Open with: "What stuck with you overnight?" — Let people share freely
  • Review the Sprint Events Flow: Planning → Daily Scrum → Sprint Review → Retrospective
  • Discuss self-management: "Scrum Teams are cross-functional and self-managing. What does that mean in practice?"
Transition: "Today we go deeper into the planning side of Scrum."

8:45 – 9:30 — Planning with Scrum

45 min

Talking Points

  • Estimation: Relative sizing, not absolute. "Is this bigger or smaller than that?" T-shirt sizes (XS, S, M, L, XL) as an accessible technique.
  • Product Backlog Refinement: Ongoing activity, not an event. Break big items down, add detail, re-order. The Scrum Team decides how and when refinement is done — this is no longer prescribed as a specific percentage of Sprint capacity.
  • Definition of Ready: Clear, Sized, Testable. An item should meet these criteria before entering Sprint Planning.
  • Monitoring Sprint Progress: Burndown/burnup charts, transparency of work remaining.

Estimation Exercise (10 min)

Materials: Sticky notes or index cards with work items

  • Prepare 8–10 work items relevant to their context
  • Teams estimate using T-shirt sizes
  • Compare and discuss differences — the conversation matters more than the number
Emphasize that estimation is about shared understanding, not precision. "The conversation is the value, not the number."
Transition: "Time for a break, then we'll put this into practice with a new exercise."
9:30 – 9:45 — Break (15 min)

9:45 – 10:15 — Presentation Sprint Setup

30 min

Talking Points

  • The Challenge: Build a presentation selling their product vision
  • Two Sprints to create it; teams pick their own PO this time
  • What to include: problem statement, value proposition, target audience, how it works, call to action

Setup

Materials: Paper, markers, poster boards, phones for reference

  • Distribute materials to each team
  • Each team selects their own Product Owner
  • Teams define their own Definition of Done for the presentation
  • Remind them to use their Scrum boards
Transition: "You have 80 minutes for Sprint 1. Go."

10:15 – 11:15 — Presentation Sprint 1 & Debrief

60 min
  • Planning (5 min): Teams decide what they'll accomplish in this Sprint
  • Create (20 min): Teams work on their presentation
  • Review (10 min): Each team shows progress; other teams provide feedback too
  • Retro (5 min): Quick reflection on what to improve for Sprint 2

Remaining time is buffer for transitions and discussions.

This sprint is more open-ended than the Lego exercise. Let teams find their own rhythm. Observe how they apply what they learned yesterday.
Transition: "Lunch break. Sprint 2 starts right after."
11:15 – 12:30 — Lunch (75 min)

12:30 – 1:30 — Presentation Sprint 2

60 min
  • Planning (5 min): Incorporate feedback from Sprint 1 Review
  • Create (20 min): Polish and complete the presentation
  • Review (10 min): Final iteration feedback
  • Retro (5 min): Reflect on the full journey

Remaining time is buffer for transitions and final prep.

Transition: "Now it's showtime. Final presentations."
1:30 – 1:45 — Break (15 min)

1:45 – 2:30 — Final Presentations & Debrief

45 min

Talking Points

  • This IS the final Sprint Review — frame it that way
  • Each team gets 10–15 minutes to present
  • Encourage feedback from other teams after each presentation
  • Celebrate the work and the improvement from Sprint 1 to the final product
Make this celebratory. The point is not perfection — it's the iterative improvement and the team's growth through the process.
Transition: "You've now experienced Scrum firsthand. Let's talk about taking it home."

2:30 – 4:00 — Getting Started, Appreciation & Closing

90 min

Talking Points

  • "Should We Use Scrum?" discussion canvas — Facilitate an honest conversation. Scrum isn't for everything. Where does it fit in their work?
  • Identify Scrum Team roles — If they were to start a Scrum Team tomorrow, who would fill each accountability?

Appreciation Wall

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Each person writes an appreciation for someone else in the workshop
  • Post on the wall; read a few aloud

Wrap-Up

  • Suggested reading: Scrum: The Art of Doing Twice the Work in Half the Time by Jeff Sutherland; the Scrum Guide (scrum.org)
