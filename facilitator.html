Key Quotes

Shu Ha Ri

In the Shu state you know all the rules and the forms. You repeat them so your body absorbs them. You don’t deviate at all. In the Ha state, once you’ve mastered the forms, you can make innovations. In the Ri state you’re able to discard the forms; you’ve truly mastered the practice, and you’re able to be creative in an unhindered way, because the knowledge is so deeply embedded in you, your every step expresses its essence.

— Jeff Sutherland, Scrum: The Art of Doing Twice the Work in Half the Time

DAY 1

Wednesday, March 18, 2026

9:00 – 9:45 — Kickoff

45 min

Talking Points

  • Welcome everyone; set an informal, energetic tone
  • Introductions round: each person shares name, role, and one creative project they’re proud of — from Artwod or their own practice
  • Frame the two days: participatory, hands-on, safe space to experiment

Working Agreements (10 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Each person writes 1–2 ground rules on sticky notes (one idea per note)
  • Post on wall, cluster similar ones, read aloud
  • Group agrees to honor them for both days — this is a social contract
  • Pre-seeded examples on slide (“Phones on silent,” “Be present,” “No bad ideas,” “Have fun”) are prompts, not prescriptions — let the group own it

"Great Team" Exercise (15 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers, wall space

  • Prompt: “Think of an AMAZING team you were on — a collaborative art project, a product team, a band, a theater production — any group. What made it great?”
  • One characteristic per sticky note
  • Post and cluster into themes on the wall

Common themes that always emerge:

  • Trust — "We trusted each other," "Had each other's backs"
  • Communication / Openness — "We talked openly," "No hidden agendas"
  • Shared Purpose — "We all knew what we were doing and why"
  • Fun / Enjoyment — "It was fun," "Energy was high"
  • Commitment — "Everyone pulled their weight"
  • Diverse Skills — "Different skills," "We complemented each other"

Debrief: “Notice how almost everything you wrote is about people and interactions — not about having the right tools or the right process.” This foreshadows the Agile Manifesto — plant the seed, don’t reveal it yet.

Set the tone early — this is participatory, not a lecture. If you're talking for more than 3 minutes straight, pause and ask a question.
Transition: "Now that we know what makes great teams, let's look at a framework designed to help teams be great."

9:45 – 10:30 — Fundamentals

45 min

Talking Points

  • Agile Manifesto values — Don’t just read them. Note: the slide layout uses top/bottom (not left/right), so the subtitle reads “While there is value in the items below, we value the items above more.” The speaker notes include a prompt: “Do you see how these map back to what makes a great team?” — use this to connect the Manifesto to the Great Team exercise.
  • Bridge slide: “Agile is a statement of values. How do you practice them?” — this transitions from values into the Scrum framework as one way to put them into practice.
  • Section Divider: Fundamentals of Scrum — marks the shift from Agile values to the Scrum framework itself.
  • What Is a Scrum? — The “rugby scrum” metaphor from Takeuchi & Nonaka (1986): the team moves the ball forward as a unit, no handoffs, no sequential phases. Key point: “The team decides!” — the team decides how the work gets done, not management.
  • What Is Scrum? — Use the Scrum Guide definition: “a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems.” The slide also shows a visible DISCUSS question: “What makes a problem ‘complex’ vs. just ‘complicated’?”
  • Stacey Matrix — The Stacey Matrix shows how complexity increases as requirements and technology become less certain. Point out the four zones: Simple, Complicated, Complex, Chaotic. Ask participants where their current work falls. Scrum is designed for the Complex zone — where more is unknown than known. The slide also has a visible THINK & WRITE prompt: “What is one task from your work that falls into each zone?” Use this first, then follow up with the verbal discussion question: “Where does your current work fall on this map?”
  • Cynefin Framework — Cynefin (pronounced "kuh-NEV-in") provides a decision-making framework. Each domain has a different response pattern:
    • Complex: Probe → Sense → Respond (this is where Scrum operates)
    • Complicated: Sense → Analyze → Respond
    • Clear: Sense → Categorize → Respond
    • Chaotic: Act → Sense → Respond
    The key insight: in complex work, you can't analyze your way to the answer — you have to experiment.

    What does “Emergent Practice” mean? In complicated work, the right approach already exists — find the expert. In complex work, the right approach doesn’t exist yet — it emerges from trying things. You can’t know the answer until you act. Example: wiring a building is complicated (call an electrician). Designing a new course for learners you’ve never taught is complex (you won’t know if it works until you ship it). Connect to the Manifesto: “Responding to Change over Following a Plan” — this is WHY.
    Discussion question (prompt verbally — not on slide): "How does each domain change the way you should make decisions?"
  • Three Pillars of Empiricism — Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation. These are the foundation everything else rests on.
  • Empiricism in Practice table is in the appendix — use as a reference or Day 2 exercise if time allows.

"What Is YOUR Product?" (3 min)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers

  • After the “What is a Product?” discussion slide, prompt participants to answer three questions on a sticky note: What value does your product deliver? Who are your users, customers, and stakeholders? What makes it worth working on?
  • Start a 3-minute timer
  • Invite a few volunteers to share with the room
  • Important: Have them keep the sticky note — it comes back on Day 2.
Keep this section interactive. Ask questions, don't lecture. Every concept should connect to something they already know.
“Plans are worthless, but planning is everything.” — Dwight D. Eisenhower. Use this quote when discussing why Scrum embraces change rather than fighting it.
Transition: "These principles need a structure. Let's look at how Scrum puts them into practice."
10:30 – 10:45 — Break (15 min)

10:45 – 11:15 — The Framework

30 min

Talking Points

  • Scrum Framework overview — Show the big picture diagram. Everything fits inside the Sprint container.
  • Three accountabilities:
    • Product Owner — maximizes value, owns the Product Backlog
    • Developers — create the Increment each Sprint
    • Scrum Master — serves the team, removes impediments, coaches Scrum
  • The Sprint — The Sprint is itself an event — a fixed-length container (1 month or less) for all other events. Everything happens inside Sprints.
  • Events overview — Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective
  • Artifacts & Commitments — Product Backlog (Product Goal), Sprint Backlog (Sprint Goal), Increment (Definition of Done)
  • Definition of Done — The shared quality standard. If it doesn’t meet the DoD, it’s not done. Visible DISCUSS question: “What does ‘Done’ look like for your team’s work right now?”

Match the Accountability (interactive slide)

  • The slide has a drag-and-drop exercise: participants match scenario cards to the correct Scrum accountability (PO, Developers, Scrum Master)
  • Let participants discuss and sort the cards before revealing the answers
  • Use the Check Answers button on the slide to reveal correct placements
Visible DISCUSS questions on the framework slides to use as prompts:
  • Product Owner: “Who in your organization is closest to this role today?”
  • Developers: “In Scrum, ‘Developers’ means anyone doing the work — not just coders. Who are the developers on your team?”
  • The Sprint: “Imagine your team had to ship a finished piece of course content every two weeks. What would you do differently?”
Connect each concept to their Artwod context. Ask: “Who plays these roles in your work now?” and “What would a Sprint look like for your team?” The Scrum Master slide includes an Artwod-specific prompt: “Think about the last time a project stalled at Artwod. What was actually blocking progress — and whose job was it to remove that block?”
Transition: "You've heard the theory. Now let's experience it. Time to build something."

11:15 – 12:00 — Lego Pre-game

45 min

Talking Points

  • Transition from theory to practice: “You’ve learned the framework. Now you’re going to experience it.”
  • Quote: “In theory there is no difference between a theory and a practice, but in practice there is.”

Setting the Stage (~3 min)

  • Explain why Lego: they’re about to experience everything they just discussed
  • A PO will pitch a vision, they’ll form teams, discover what to build, and then build it in 3 Sprints

Form Teams + Select Scrum Masters (~5 min)

  • Self-organize into 2 teams
  • Each team selects their own Scrum Master
  • SM coaches and removes impediments — does NOT manage or assign work
  • Watch for: teams struggling to self-organize (this is itself a lesson)

Vision Pitch (~5 min)

  • PUT ON YOUR PO HAT. Say: “I’m now speaking as your Product Owner.”
  • Pitch your city vision LIVE — don’t read from slides
  • A good vision has: compelling purpose (WHY), users (WHO), needs (WHAT)
  • Stay deliberately vague on specifics. “Impress me.”
  • After pitching, ask: “How does it feel hearing this vision?”

Sprint Rules Overview (~2 min)

  • 3 Sprints: Planning (5 min), Build (8 min), Review (7 min), Retro (10 min)
  • Both teams build ONE city — they must coordinate
  • Only items accepted by the PO in Review count as Done

Materials Checklist for This Block

  • Sticky notes (lots, multiple colors), markers
  • Painter’s tape for wall
  • Large paper for Scrum boards (or use whiteboard)
Buffer time before lunch. Use it for questions or let teams settle in. Product discovery happens AFTER lunch — don’t start backlog creation now.
Transition: “Enjoy lunch. When you come back, we discover what to build — then we build it.”
12:00 – 12:45 — Lunch (45 min)
During lunch: Set up the product discovery wall (large paper or cleared wall space for sticky notes). Set up Sprint Backlog areas for each team (tape columns on wall or use Scrum board handout). Put out Lego boxes.

12:45 – 1:25 — Product Discovery

40 min

What We’re Building: A Product Board

This is a simplified version of User Story Mapping (per Lego4Scrum book, pp. 59–67). We don’t use that label — we just call it product discovery. The wall becomes a “Product Board”: a shared understanding of the product.

The wall reads left to right: Users → Needs → Features. Features become the Product Backlog.

Materials: Three colors of sticky notes (one per layer), markers, large wall space.

Step 1: Who Are Our Users? (~5 min)

  • Ask: “Who will live in, visit, and work in this city?”
  • This should come directly from the vision you just pitched
  • Identify 2–3 user types (personas). For Art City: artists/creators (primary), visitors/art lovers, families/residents
  • Write each on a LARGE sticky note on the LEFT side of the wall
  • Then ask: “Which user is most important?” — prioritize users BEFORE needs. If artists don’t come, nothing else matters. Start with the primary user.

Step 2: What Do They Need? (~15 min)

  • Start with the primary user. Ask: “Imagine a day in the life of an artist arriving in Art City for the first time. From the moment they arrive — what do they need?”
  • Write one need per sticky note (different color), place in the CENTER of the wall next to the user
  • CRITICAL — DEFER FEATURES: When someone says “a house!” or “a studio!” — redirect: “That’s a building. What NEED does it serve? The artist needs a place to create — that’s the need. A studio is one way to address it.”
  • Needs sound like: a place to create, a way to show their work, somewhere to live, a place to eat, community and connection, inspiration
  • Then do the same for secondary users (visitors, families)
  • 10 min writing + 5 min discussing/grouping
Per Krivitsky: “We tend to go too fast into feature-ideation mode without really understanding our users.” Spending time on needs IS the teaching moment. Don’t rush to features.

Step 3: What Do We Build? (~10 min)

  • NOW say: “OK, now turn those needs into things we can actually build with Lego.”
  • Write one feature per sticky note (third color) on the RIGHT side of the wall, next to the need it addresses
  • The wall now reads left to right: Users → Needs → Features
  • These features ARE the Product Backlog — created collaboratively, not handed to them
  • Per the book: “Bringing a printed ready-made backlog is just as bad as writing specifications and sending them by email.”

Step 4: Estimate & Prioritize (~10 min)

  • Silent sorting (2–3 min): Representatives sort features by T-shirt size (XS, S, M, L, XL) without talking. Move items between columns.
  • PO re-prioritizes: During a short break, arrange the estimated backlog by business value
  • PO logic: infrastructure/landscape first (understand the layout), simple items first (learn to build properly), complex items later
  • Per the book: “The conversation matters more than the number.”
Three colors, three layers. Use different sticky note colors for Users, Needs, and Features so the wall is visually clear. The Product Board stays on the wall through all 3 sprints — teams pull from it during Sprint Planning.
Transition: “You know who you’re building for, what they need, and what to build. Time to sprint.”

1:25 – 2:25 — Lego Sprints 1 & 2

60 min

PO Behavior Cheat Sheet

Explicitly separate your trainer hat from your PO hat: “I’m now speaking as your Product Owner.”

Your vision: ART CITY — a city where artists of all kinds can come to live, learn, work, display, and appreciate art. An artist’s dream city. A place to raise a family. The PO pitch: “Build me a city where artists come to live their best creative life. A place where you’d want to move your family.”

Hidden acceptance criteria (do NOT volunteer — only reveal if teams ASK specific questions):

  • Does it inspire creativity? — An artist’s city should feel creative, not corporate
  • Can you see where art happens? — Studios, galleries, outdoor spaces for making and showing
  • Is there community? — Artists need each other: shared spaces, gathering places
  • Does it feel livable? — Artists LIVE here: homes, food, places to raise families
  • Is it cohesive? — One city, not a random collection of buildings
  • Is there beauty in the details? — These are artists. The city itself should be art.

If asked “What do you want?”: “Build me a city where artists come to live their best creative life.”

Sprint 1 disappointment: “Where do I make art? Where do I show my work? I see buildings but I don’t see a city for artists. This could be any city.”

Frame rejections as disappointment, not criticism: “This isn’t what I envisioned” not “This is bad.”

Key PO questions during Review:

  • “Where is my city?!”
  • “Where do I make art? Where do I show my work?”
  • “Imagine you’re an artist arriving here for the first time…”
  • “Could I raise a family here?”
  • “What is missing? What doesn’t belong?”

Sprint 1 — Let Them Fail (~30 min)

  • Planning (5 min): Everyone to the board — teams pull items from Product Backlog into Sprint Backlogs. Coordinate across teams — one city. Confidence vote (fist of five). Watch for: teams not coordinating, SMs planning instead of devs, no one asking PO questions.
  • Build (8 min): Start timer. Leave room for 2–3 min (“business trip”). Note who builds alone vs coordinates, whether anyone asks PO questions (probably not). End at exactly 8 min: “Hands up!”
  • Review (7 min): “WHERE IS MY CITY?!” Demand integration — 95% of teams won’t have integrated. Inspect and Adapt, not Inspect and Accept. Be disappointed, not mean. “Did you ask me what I wanted?” Reject items that don’t meet hidden criteria.
  • Retro (10 min): Team retro 5 min + Overall retro 5 min. THIS IS WHERE DEFINITION OF DONE EMERGES. Ask: “How would you know if something is done BEFORE showing it to me?” Have teams create their Definition of Done on a sticky note. Push for actionable items: “Show PO work at 4-min mark” not “communicate more.”
Sprint 1 will be chaos. That’s the point. Do NOT soften your feedback. The contrast between Sprint 1 and Sprint 3 IS the learning.

Sprint 2 — Coach Them (~30 min)

  • Planning (5 min): Watch for teams referencing retro actions, using their Definition of Done, asking PO questions. Confidence vote.
  • Build (8 min): Be available but don’t volunteer criteria. Note improvements from Sprint 1.
  • Review (7 min): “WHERE IS MY CITY?!” Accept improved work that meets criteria. Still reject items that miss criteria — but explain why. Acknowledge progress. Update velocity tally on wall.
  • Retro (10 min): Team retro 5 min + Overall retro 5 min. Push for actionable items only. “Communicate more” is not an action. “What specific thing will you do differently in the next 8 minutes?”
Watch for improvements in communication, planning, and quality. Note these for the debrief.
2:25 – 2:40 — Break (15 min)

2:40 – 3:10 — Lego Sprint 3

30 min

Sprint 3 — Celebrate (~30 min)

  • Planning (5 min): Teams should be confident and realistic. Be fully available. If injecting surprise: “We’re hosting a visiting artist. Outdoor sculpture — top priority!”
  • Build (8 min): Stay present. If team finishes early, they can pull more items from Product Backlog (pull-based flow).
  • Review (7 min): “WHERE IS MY CITY?!” Accept most work. Have teams physically assemble pieces into one city. Celebrate the contrast with Sprint 1. Update velocity on wall — the improvement arc tells the story.
  • Retro (10 min): Team retro 5 min + Overall retro 5 min. Focus on the full journey: “Compare Sprint 1 vs Sprint 3. What changed?”

3:10 – 3:30 — Debrief & Values

20 min

Debrief (20 min)

Observe / Learn / Connect (5 min write + 15 min discuss)

Materials: Sticky notes, markers

Each person writes one sticky note per category:

  • Observe: “Write one thing you noticed change across the Sprints.”
  • Learn: “Write the biggest lesson from the simulation.”
  • Connect: “Write one way this connects to your real work.”

Post notes together, then debrief as a group.

Discussion Prompts

  • “What patterns emerged across the three Sprints?”
  • “When did you start asking the Product Owner questions? Why not earlier?”
  • “How did it feel when I rejected your work?” → connects to stakeholder feedback
  • “What assumptions did you make in Sprint 1 that turned out wrong?”
  • “If you could do it again, what would you do differently in the first 5 minutes?”
Virginia Satir Change Model (mention verbally, 2–3 min): What they experienced follows a known pattern — Status Quo → Foreign Element (Sprint 1 chaos) → Resistance → Transforming Idea (retro insights) → Practice → New Status Quo (Sprint 3 flow). “The discomfort you felt in Sprint 1 is normal. You already made it through in 90 minutes.”
This debrief is the most important part of the entire simulation. Do not rush it. Let the group make the connections themselves.

Scrum Values (fold into debrief)

  • Five values: Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage
  • Don’t just list them — connect each value to the Lego experience
  • Verbal discussion question: “Which came naturally? Which was hardest?”
  • Keep this conversational — no sticky note exercise, just the Five Values slide with group discussion
Transition: “Let’s put some language around what we experienced, then close out the day.”

10:15 – 10:45 — The Challenge: “The Artwod Way”

30 min

PO Vision Pitch (~5 min)

PUT ON YOUR PO HAT: “I’m now speaking as your Product Owner.”

Pitch: “Build me a Scrum presentation — big, visual, on these sheets — that any Artwod team member, current or future, can look at and understand how we work together. Not generic Scrum theory from the internet. OUR Scrum. How THIS team runs Sprints. Who does what. What we build and how we ship it. And it should look like something that came from Artwod — your art, your style, your identity. Two teams, one presentation.”

Format: Large sheets of paper. Physical. Visual. Illustrated with Artwod artists’ art. Something you’d hang on the office wall.

Required Sections (state these explicitly)

The presentation MUST include:

  1. Why Scrum? — Why did Artwod choose this way of working?
  2. Our Team — Who fills each accountability? PO, SM, Developers. Real names.
  3. How a Sprint Works — What events, what order, how long? Our Sprint length.
  4. Our Product Backlog — The first 5 real items we’ll work on. Not workshop items — real Artwod work.
  5. Our Definition of Done — When is work truly finished at Artwod?
  6. When We Start — The date of our first real Sprint.

These 6 sections ARE the Startup Kit — they’re building it as a presentation.

Definition of Done (TEAM creates this, not PO)

Day 1 lesson: Without a clear DoD, your acceptance criteria felt arbitrary. Don’t repeat that.

Prompt the team: “Yesterday we didn’t have a clear Definition of Done and it caused problems. Before you start — what does Done mean for THIS product? Agree as a team and write it down.”

If they struggle, ask: “How would you know a section is finished before showing it to me?”

What you hope they arrive at (don’t dictate, but coach toward):

  • Understandable by someone who wasn’t here
  • Includes Artwod artwork, not just text
  • Integrates with the other team’s sections
  • PO-accepted

If they write a weak DoD, let Sprint 1 Review expose it — same pattern as Day 1 Lego.

Product Discovery (~15 min)

Same flow as Day 1 but faster — they’ve done this. Users (left) → Needs (center) → Sections (right).

  • Users: current team (primary), future hires (secondary), leadership (tertiary)
  • Needs: what does each person need from this presentation?
  • Sections: map to the 6 required sections above — teams split the work

Estimate & Prioritize (~5 min)

  • You (PO) decide what’s most important for Sprint 1
  • What creates value fastest? What would make the biggest impact if shown first?

Sprint Rules: Plan 10, Create 35, Review 15, Retro 10 (3 Sprints x 70 min)

Transition: “Self-organize into two teams. Remember: one product, two teams. Check the Definition of Done on the whiteboard.”
