Fundamentals of Scrum

What Is Scrum?

“Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems.”

— The Scrum Guide (2020)

  • Lightweight: Few rules, easy to understand — hard to master
  • Framework, not a process: Provides structure; your team fills in the practices
  • Adaptive & Complex: Built for work where requirements emerge and change
What makes a problem “complex” vs. just “complicated”?
Scrum solves the problem of helping people understand that the way they work by default is the reason for most project failures. Seeing this and understanding it unlocks the way for you to consider a new way of working. We want to answer: how do people ACTUALLY work? This happens in teams that want to create something new. Why did it take so long and take so much effort?

The Complexity of Product Development

Simple

Everything is known — follow the recipe

Complicated

More is known than unknown — call the experts

Complex

More is unknown than known — experiment & adapt

Chaotic

Very little is known — act first, make sense later

DISCUSS: Where does your current work fall on this map? Waterfall and Gantt Charts — this was a World War I organizational idea. Once the plan hits reality it falls apart. People are hired to update the chart — people are being paid to lie to them. Reality versus the plan — we must look at actual development. "Plans are worthless, but planning is everything." Management wants two things: control and predictability — leads to graphs and charts. All that wanting to know the unknowable doesn't work because every project involves the discovery of problems and bursts of inspiration. Trying to restrict that to charts is doomed to failure. It's not how people work, not how progress happens, not how ideas reach fruition and how great things are made. It leads to frustrated people who don't get what they want.

The Cynefin Framework

DISCUSS: How does each domain change the way you should make decisions? The Cynefin framework helps teams recognize what kind of problem they're facing so they can respond appropriately. In the Complex domain (where Scrum lives), you can't analyze your way to an answer — you have to probe (try something), sense (see what happens), and respond (adapt). This is fundamentally different from Complicated problems where experts can analyze and predict.

How Scrum Events Use Empiricism

Every Scrum event is an opportunity to inspect and adapt.

Event Transparency Inspection Adaptation
Sprint Planning Backlog is visible Examine what can be done Create the Sprint plan
Daily Scrum Progress is shared Check progress toward goal Adjust the day’s plan
Sprint Review Increment is shown Evaluate what was built Update the Backlog
Retrospective Process is discussed Examine how the team worked Commit to improvements
Which of these three pillars does your team practice today — and which one is missing?
Inspect and adapt. Respect, honesty, discipline, thought, introspection is required to do this. End goal: teams improve their productivity and get what they want.

The Scrum Framework

Fitting the Pieces Together

One Scrum Team · Three accountabilities · Five events · Three artifacts

Framework for team performance: how does the team work together? Careful alignment. Unity of purpose. Clarity of goal. The Sprint is a container event — everything happens within it. Notice how the feedback loop from Review goes all the way back to the Product Backlog.

Product Owner

Maximizes the value of the product

  • Developing and communicating the Product Goal
  • Creating and clearly ordering the Product Backlog
  • Ensuring the Product Backlog is transparent, visible, and understood
  • Representing stakeholder needs
Who in your organization is closest to this role today?
What brings the most value? 80% of the value of software is in 20% of the features. The PO's job is to find that 20%.

Developers

Turn Product Backlog items into a usable Increment

  • Creating the Sprint Backlog (a plan for the Sprint)
  • Instilling quality by adhering to the Definition of Done
  • Adapting their plan each day toward the Sprint Goal
  • Holding each other accountable as professionals
In Scrum, “Developers” means anyone doing the work — not just coders. Who are the developers on your team?

Scrum Master

Accountable for establishing Scrum as defined in the Scrum Guide

  • Coaching the team in self-management and cross-functionality
  • Helping focus on creating high-value Increments that meet the DoD
  • Causing the removal of impediments
  • Ensuring events happen and are positive, productive, and timeboxed
What are the biggest impediments your team faces today?
Toyota Production System: production should flow swiftly and calmly throughout the process, and one of management's key tasks is to remove impediments to that flow. Everything that stands in the way is waste, and this is morally wrong. Eliminating waste is the first responsibility of management. This requires being honest with yourselves.

The Sprint

The Sprint is itself an event — a fixed-length container (1 month or less) for all other events

  • No changes that endanger the Sprint Goal
  • Quality does not decrease
  • The Product Backlog is refined as needed
  • Scope may be clarified and renegotiated with the PO
What would change if your team committed to delivering something every two weeks?
Scrum sets sequential goals — a finished increment of product. Near real-time feedback. These are cycles. The team decides. They create a sense of their own velocity. How can we work together better in the next Sprint? Measure the velocity.

Artifacts & Commitments

Product Backlog

Commitment: Product Goal

An ordered list of everything needed to improve the product

Sprint Backlog

Commitment: Sprint Goal

The Sprint Goal + selected PBIs + plan for delivering them

Increment

Commitment: Definition of Done

A concrete stepping stone toward the Product Goal

Definition of Done

“A formal description of the state of the Increment when it meets the quality measures required for the product.”

— Scrum Guide (2020)

Why it matters

  • Creates transparency — everyone knows what “Done” means
  • Ensures quality and consistency across Increments
  • If multiple teams work on one product, they must share a DoD
What does “Done” look like for your team’s work right now?

The Sprint as a Learning Loop

Each event is an opportunity to inspect and adapt — the heartbeat of empiricism.

Self-Managing Teams

The team decides WHO does WHAT and HOW

  • No one tells the team how to turn backlog items into Increments
  • The team collectively owns the Sprint Backlog
  • Decisions are made by those closest to the work

“Scrum Teams are cross-functional, meaning the members have all the skills necessary to create value each Sprint.”

— Scrum Guide (2020)

The team decides. They create a sense of their own velocity. How can we work together better in the next Sprint?

Planning with Scrum

Estimation

Estimation is a team activity, not a management tool.

Relative Sizing

Compare items to each other, not to hours. Use T-shirt sizes: XS, S, M, L, XL

Why Relative?

Humans are bad at absolute estimates. We’re great at comparing: “This is about twice as big as that.”

Estimation Exercise

As a team, estimate these items from your domain using T-shirt sizes (XS, S, M, L, XL):

  • Pick 5–8 real tasks from your current work
  • Discuss as a team — the conversation IS the value
  • Time: 10 minutes

Product Backlog Refinement

  • An ongoing activity, NOT a formal Scrum event
  • Adding detail, estimates, and order to items
  • The Scrum Team decides how and when refinement is done
  • Makes future Sprint Planning smoother

Think of it as “grooming the queue” — keeping the backlog ready for the next Sprint.

When Is a Backlog Item “Ready”?

Clear

The team understands what is being asked

Sized

The team has estimated the relative effort

Testable

The team knows how to verify it’s done

Ready ≠ fully specified. Just enough to start.

Monitoring Sprint Progress

  • The Sprint Backlog makes progress visible
  • Developers inspect progress at least daily (Daily Scrum)
  • Burndown/burnup charts can help — but they’re tools, not mandates
  • The Sprint Goal is the measure of success, not individual task completion

Presentation Sprint

Sell Your Vision

The Challenge

Build a presentation that sells the vision of your product to stakeholders.

  • You have 2 Sprints
  • Teams create their own Product Backlog
  • A team member becomes Product Owner
  • Materials: paper, markers, poster boards. Phones for reference.

What to Include

  • Problem statement — What pain does your product solve?
  • Value proposition — Why is YOUR solution the best?
  • Target audience — Who are your users?
  • How it works — Show, don’t just tell
  • Call to action — What do you want stakeholders to do?

Sprint Planning

  1. Select a Product Owner for your team
  2. Build your Product Backlog (what goes into the presentation?)
  3. Select items for this Sprint

Time: 5 minutes

Create!

20:00

CREATE! Build your presentation.

Sprint Review

  • Show what you have so far
  • Get feedback from the PO and other teams
  • Is any part Done? What needs more work?
  • Adapt your Product Backlog based on feedback

Sprint Retrospective

What went well?

What could improve?

What will we change?

Quick retro — 5 minutes. Then Sprint 2!
