Where Does “Scrum” Come From?

“The ‘rugby approach’ — where a team tries to go the distance as a unit, passing the ball back and forth — may better serve today’s competitive requirements.”

— Takeuchi & Nonaka, “The New New Product Development Game” (Harvard Business Review, 1986)

The Rugby Metaphor

In rugby, a scrum is when the whole team locks together and moves the ball forward as one unit — no handoffs, no sequential phases.

From Paper to Practice

Jeff Sutherland and Ken Schwaber took this idea and created the Scrum framework in the early 1990s, first presented at OOPSLA in 1995.

How is your team’s current way of working more like a relay race or more like a rugby scrum?
The key insight from Takeuchi & Nonaka: the best teams don't work in sequential phases (like a relay race). They work together, overlapping phases, adapting as they go. This is the fundamental shift Scrum asks teams to make. The relay race metaphor is powerful for this audience — art departments often work sequentially (design → review → revise → approve).

What Is Scrum?

“Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems.”

— The Scrum Guide (2020)

  • Lightweight: Few rules, easy to understand — hard to master
  • Framework, not a process: Provides structure; your team fills in the practices
  • Adaptive & Complex: Built for work where requirements emerge and change
What makes a problem “complex” vs. just “complicated”?
Scrum solves the problem of helping people understand that the way they work by default is the reason for most project failures. Seeing this and understanding it unlocks the way for you to consider a new way of working. We want to answer: how do people ACTUALLY work? This happens in teams that want to create something new. Why did it take so long and take so much effort?

The Cynefin Framework

DISCUSS: How does each domain change the way you should make decisions? The Cynefin framework helps teams recognize what kind of problem they're facing so they can respond appropriately. In the Complex domain (where Scrum lives), you can't analyze your way to an answer — you have to probe (try something), sense (see what happens), and respond (adapt). Fitting the Pieces Together

Framework for team performance: how does the team work together? Careful alignment. Unity of purpose. Clarity of goal. The Sprint is a container event — everything happens within it. Notice how the feedback loop from Review goes all the way back to the Product Backlog.

Product Owner

Maximizes the value of the product

  • Developing and communicating the Product Goal
  • Creating and clearly ordering the Product Backlog
  • Ensuring the Product Backlog is transparent, visible, and understood
  • Representing stakeholder needs
Who in your organization is closest to this role today?
What brings the most value? 80% of the value of software is in 20% of the features. The PO's job is to find that 20%.

Developers

Turn Product Backlog items into a usable Increment (the teachers, designers, and coordinators who do the hands-on work)

  • Creating the Sprint Backlog (a plan for the Sprint)
  • Instilling quality by adhering to the Definition of Done
  • Adapting their plan each day toward the Sprint Goal
  • Holding each other accountable as professionals
In Scrum, “Developers” means anyone doing the work — not just coders. Who are the developers on your team?

Scrum Master

Accountable for establishing Scrum as defined in the Scrum Guide

  • Coaching the team in self-management and cross-functionality
  • Helping focus on creating high-value Increments that meet the DoD
  • Causing the removal of impediments
  • Ensuring events happen and are positive, productive, and timeboxed
Think about the last time a student project stalled. What was actually blocking them — and whose job was it to remove that block?

Everything that stands in the way is waste, and this is morally wrong. Eliminating waste is the first responsibility of management. This requires being honest with yourselves.

The Sprint

The Sprint is itself an event — a fixed-length container (1 month or less) for all other events

  • No changes that endanger the Sprint Goal
  • Quality does not decrease
  • The Product Backlog is refined as needed
  • Scope may be clarified and renegotiated with the PO
Imagine your department had to show a finished piece of curriculum every two weeks instead of finalizing it all at semester’s end. What would you do differently?
Scrum sets sequential goals — a finished increment of product. Near real-time feedback. These are cycles. The team decides. They create a sense of their own velocity. How can we work together better in the next Sprint? Measure the velocity.

Estimation

Estimation is a team activity, not a management tool.

Relative Sizing

Compare items to each other, not to hours. Use T-shirt sizes: XS, S, M, L, XL

Why Relative?

Humans are bad at absolute estimates. We’re great at comparing: “This is about twice as big as that.”

- Key point: estimation is a team activity, not a management tool - Relative sizing: "Is this bigger or smaller than that?" - T-shirt sizes (XS, S, M, L, XL) are accessible for non-technical teams - The conversation matters more than the number

Product Backlog Refinement

  • An ongoing activity, NOT a formal Scrum event
  • Adding detail, estimates, and order to items
  • The Scrum Team decides how and when refinement is done
  • Makes future Sprint Planning smoother

Think of it as “grooming the queue” — keeping the backlog ready for the next Sprint.

- Refinement is ongoing, NOT a formal Scrum event - Break big items down, add detail, re-order - The Scrum Team decides how and when refinement is done - Connect to Lego: "Remember how Sprint 2 planning was faster? That's refinement in action."

When Is a Backlog Item “Ready”?

Clear

The team understands what is being asked

Sized

The team has estimated the relative effort

Testable

You can describe what ‘finished’ looks like — like a rubric for a student project

Ready ≠ fully specified. Just enough to start.

- Definition of Ready: Clear, Sized, Testable - Compare to Definition of Done — Ready is the entry gate, Done is the exit gate - "Like a rubric for a student project" — connect to their teaching context - Not in the Scrum Guide, but a useful team practice

A team member becomes Product Owner
Materials: paper, markers, poster boards
- Teams pick their own PO this time (callback to Lego where you were PO) - Emphasize: agree on a Definition of Done before starting (callback to Lego DoD) - 2 Sprints to create the presentation - Teams create their own Product Backlog — they own the process now

What to Include

  • Problem statement — What pain does your product solve?
  • Value proposition — Why is YOUR solution the best?
  • Target audience — Who are your users?
  • How it works — Show, don’t just tell
  • Call to action — What do you want stakeholders to do?
- These are guidelines, not requirements — teams can adapt - Encourage "show, don't tell" — visual aids, demos, mockups - Teams should break these into backlog items for Sprint Planning

Sprint Planning

  1. Select a Product Owner for your team
  2. Build your Product Backlog (what goes into the presentation?)
  3. Select items for this Sprint

Time: 5 minutes

- Each team selects their own PO — this person owns the vision - Teams define their own Definition of Done for the presentation (callback to Lego) - Remind teams to use their Scrum boards from yesterday - Encourage teams to write backlog items on sticky notes

Create!

20:00

CREATE! Build your presentation.

- 20-minute timebox — click Start on the timer - Circulate and observe; don't intervene unless teams are stuck - Watch for teams applying Lego lessons: asking PO questions, updating boards - Note positive patterns to call out in the debrief

Sprint Review

  • Show what you have so far
  • Get feedback from the PO and other teams
  • Is any part Done? What needs more work?
  • Adapt your Product Backlog based on feedback
- ~5 min per team; use warm/cool feedback structure - Warm: "What's working well?" Cool: "What could be stronger?" - Other teams provide feedback too — not just the PO - Teams adapt their Product Backlog based on what they hear

Sprint Retrospective

What went well?

What could improve?

What will we change?

Quick retro — 5 minutes. Then Sprint 2!

- Keep this retro short and focused — 5 minutes max - Push for actionable items, not vague goals - Ask: "What one thing will you change for Sprint 2?" - Transition quickly into Sprint 2 after lunch

Sprint 2

Incorporate your retro feedback. Refine and improve.

Sprint Planning

What will you improve? What feedback will you incorporate? (5 min)

Create

Polish and complete your presentation. (20 min)

Sprint Review

Present iteration to another team for feedback. (10 min)

Retro

Final reflection before presenting. (5 min)

Remember your Definition of Done!

This is the second iteration of the Presentation Sprint. Teams should be noticeably better at planning and incorporating feedback. Draw the parallel to the Lego sprints: "Notice how you're doing the same thing you did with Lego — inspecting and adapting."

Final Presentations

Each team: 10–15 minutes to present your product vision.

This is your final Sprint Review. Sell it!

- Frame this as the final Sprint Review — use the Scrum language - Give the audience a stakeholder role: "You are investors deciding whether to fund this" - 10-15 min per team; encourage feedback from other teams after each - Celebrate the work and the improvement from Sprint 1 to final product - Make this celebratory — the point is iterative improvement, not perfection

Getting Started with Scrum

Taking Scrum Back to Your Classroom and Department

- Transition: "You've experienced Scrum firsthand. Let's talk about taking it home." - Shift from learning to application — make it real for their context - This section should feel like a conversation, not a lecture

Should We Use Scrum?

  • What’s not working for us right now?
  • What will happen if we don’t change?
  • What do you want the future to look like?

Discuss as a group. Write answers on sticky notes or poster paper.

- Facilitate an honest conversation — Scrum isn't for everything - Use the Stacey Matrix callback: where does their work fall? - If work is complex (more unknown than known), Scrum fits well - Let participants convince each other — don't sell it yourself

Identify Your Scrum Team

Product Owner

Who understands the business, customers, and can make priority decisions?

Scrum Master

Who can coach, facilitate, and help the team grow?

Developers

Who has the skills to create the product?

For your real team back at work: Who would fill each role? Write names on sticky notes.
- Make it concrete: "If you started a Scrum Team tomorrow, who fills each role?" - Write actual names on sticky notes - Discuss: Who understands the business? Who can coach? Who does the work? - Acknowledge that one person might wear multiple hats initially

Appreciation Wall

Write a statement of appreciation about someone in this workshop or about the experience itself. Post it on the wall.

- Each person writes an appreciation for someone else in the workshop - Post on the wall; read a few aloud - This creates a positive close and reinforces the Respect value - Allow 3-5 minutes for writing and sharing
