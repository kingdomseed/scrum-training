What Is a Scrum?

In rugby, a scrum restarts play — the whole team binds together and moves the ball forward as one unit.

The term comes from Takeuchi & Nonaka's 1986 HBR paper "The New New Product Development Game." Sutherland and Schwaber built it into a framework in the early 90s, presented at OOPSLA 1995. Best teams overlap phases instead of sequential handoffs.

What Is Scrum?

“Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems.”

— The Scrum Guide (2020)

  • Lightweight: Few rules, easy to understand — hard to master
  • Framework, not a process: Provides structure; your team fills in the practices
  • Adaptive & Complex: Built for work where requirements emerge and change
"Ideas are easy, execution is hard." Scrum is how you practice these values. Toyota: "Production should flow swiftly and calmly. Removing impediments to that flow is management's key task." Framework for team performance: careful alignment, unity of purpose, clarity of goal.

What’s wrong with a well-designed plan?

“Every project involves the discovery of problems and bursts of inspiration. Trying to restrict that to charts is doomed to failure.”

— Jeff Sutherland, Scrum: The Art of Doing Twice the Work in Half the Time

~1 min. Management wants two things: control and predictability — leads to graphs and charts. Gantt charts were a WWI organizational idea. Once the plan hits reality it falls apart. "Plans are worthless but planning is everything." Transition: "Two models make this concrete."

The Complexity of Product Development

Stacey, 1996

Simple

Everything is known — follow the recipe

Complicated

More is known than unknown — call the experts

Complex

More is unknown than known — experiment & adapt

Chaotic

Very little is known — act first, make sense later

What is one task from your work that falls into each zone? Write one example for Simple, Complicated, Complex, and Chaotic.
Concrete examples for each zone — Clear: following a recipe to bake a cake. Complicated: wiring a building — call an electrician, they can analyze and solve it. Complex: designing a new course for learners you've never taught — you won't know if it works until you try it. The right approach emerges from doing. Chaotic: your server goes down during a live launch — act first, make sense later. Ask: "Where does your current work fall on this map?"

The Cynefin Framework

Don't linger — Stacey made the point, this adds the action model. Key: "Emergent practice" means the right approach reveals itself through doing, not planning. Complicated = the answer exists, find the expert. Complex = the answer doesn't exist yet, run experiments. Connect to Manifesto: "Responding to Change over Following a Plan" — this is WHY.

The Three Pillars of Empiricism

Ask: "What does empirical mean?" It means observable — based on what you can see and measure, not what you hope or plan. A thermometer is empirical — it exposes the actual temperature, not what you wish it was. The three pillars are sequential: you can't inspect what you can't see (transparency), you can't adapt without honest inspection. And for transparency you need trust — connect back to Great Team exercise. Trust was the first thing you identified. Connect forward: every Scrum event maps to these pillars (next slide).

Product Owner

Accountability 1 of 3

Maximizes the value of the product

  • Developing and communicating the Product Goal
  • Creating and clearly ordering the Product Backlog
  • Ensuring the Product Backlog is transparent, visible, and understood
  • Representing stakeholder needs
Extra data: 80% of the value of software is in 20% of the features (Standish Group). The PO's job is to find that 20%.

Developers

Accountability 2 of 3

Turn Product Backlog items into a usable Increment

  • Creating the Sprint Backlog (a plan for the Sprint)
  • Instilling quality by adhering to the Definition of Done
  • Adapting their plan each day toward the Sprint Goal
  • Holding each other accountable as professionals
In Scrum, “Developers” means anyone doing the work — not just coders. Who are the developers on your team?

Scrum Master

Accountability 3 of 3

Accountable for establishing Scrum as defined in the Scrum Guide

  • Coaching the team in self-management and cross-functionality
  • Helping focus on creating high-value Increments that meet the DoD
  • Causing the removal of impediments
  • Ensuring events happen and are positive, productive, and timeboxed
Toyota Production System: "production should flow swiftly and calmly; management's key task is to remove impediments to that flow." Everything standing in the way is waste. Connect to SM role: causing the removal of impediments.

The Sprint

The Sprint is itself an event — a fixed-length container (1 month or less) for all other events

  • No changes that endanger the Sprint Goal
  • Quality does not decrease
  • The Product Backlog is refined as needed
  • Scope may be clarified and renegotiated with the PO
Imagine your team had to ship a finished piece of course content every two weeks instead of finalizing it all at quarter’s end. What would you do differently?
Teams create a sense of their own velocity over time. Velocity is a planning tool for the team, not a management metric. Connect forward: they'll experience this in the Lego simulation.

Definition of Done

“A formal description of the state of the Increment when it meets the quality measures required for the product.”

— Scrum Guide (2020)

Why it matters

  • Creates transparency — everyone knows what “Done” means
  • Ensures quality and consistency across Increments
  • If multiple teams work on one product, they must share a DoD
What does “Done” look like for your team’s work right now?
2 min max. The real DoD learning happens during Lego Sprint 1 retro when teams feel the pain of not having one.

How Scrum Events Use Empiricism

Every Scrum event is an opportunity to inspect and adapt.

Event Transparency Inspection Adaptation
Sprint Planning Backlog is visible Examine what can be done Create the Sprint plan
Daily Scrum Progress is shared Check progress toward goal Adjust the day’s plan
Sprint Review Increment is shown Evaluate what was built Update the Backlog
Retrospective Process is discussed Examine how the team worked Commit to improvements
"Now you see how it all connects." Sutherland: "Respect, honesty, discipline, thought, introspection is required to do this." End goal: teams improve their productivity and get what they want.

Self-Managing Teams

The team decides WHO does WHAT and HOW

  • No one tells the team how to turn backlog items into Increments
  • The team collectively owns the Sprint Backlog
  • Decisions are made by those closest to the work
  • Cross-functional: The team has all skills needed — or learns them. Think “T-shaped”: deep in one area, broad enough to help across others.

“Scrum Teams are cross-functional, meaning the members have all the skills necessary to create value each Sprint.”

— Scrum Guide (2020)

Where at ArtWod do teams already self-manage? Where does someone else decide the who, what, or how?
T-shaped people: deep expertise in one area (vertical bar), broad ability to collaborate across disciplines (horizontal bar). ArtWod likely already works this way -- validate and name the pattern.

Estimation

Estimation is a team activity, not a management tool.

Relative Sizing

Compare items to each other, not to hours.

XS   S   M   L   XL

Why Relative?

Humans are bad at absolute estimates. We’re great at comparing: “This is about twice as big as that.”

The conversation matters more than the number. T-shirt sizes are accessible for non-technical teams.

Estimation Exercise

As a team, estimate these items from your domain using T-shirt sizes (XS, S, M, L, XL):

  • Pick 5–8 real tasks from your current work
  • Discuss as a team — the conversation IS the value
  • Time: 10 minutes
10 min. Debrief question: "Where did you disagree? What did the conversation reveal?" Shared understanding is the value, not precision.

Product Backlog Refinement

  • An ongoing activity, NOT a formal Scrum event
  • Adding detail, estimates, and order to items
  • The Scrum Team decides how and when refinement is done
  • Makes future Sprint Planning smoother

Think of it as “grooming the queue” — keeping the backlog ready for the next Sprint.

Connect to Lego: "Remember how Sprint 2 planning was faster? That's refinement in action."

When Is a Backlog Item “Ready”?

Clear

The team understands what is being asked

Sized

The team has estimated the relative effort

Testable

You can describe what ‘finished’ looks like — like acceptance criteria for a platform feature

Ready ≠ fully specified. Just enough to start.

Ready = entry gate, Done = exit gate. Gotcha: Definition of Ready is NOT in the Scrum Guide -- it's a useful team practice, not a requirement.

Monitoring Sprint Progress

  • The Sprint Backlog makes progress visible
  • Developers inspect progress at least daily (Daily Scrum)
  • Burndown/burnup charts can help — but they’re tools, not mandates
  • The Sprint Goal is the measure of success, not individual task completion

About the Scrum Master

The Scrum Guide defines the Scrum Master as an accountability on the Scrum Team — someone who serves the team by coaching Scrum, removing impediments, and helping the team improve.

Common approaches for teams just starting:

Internal, Part-Time

A team member takes on SM responsibilities alongside their other work. Most common in small teams.

External Coach

An experienced SM or Agile coach works with the team to get started, then transitions the role internally.

Dedicated Hire

Bringing in a full-time SM. Most investment upfront, highest expertise from day one.

What makes sense for ArtWod right now?
Be factual, not prescriptive. SM is an accountability per the Scrum Guide, not optional. SM intensity is highest early on and decreases as Scrum becomes habitual. For startups: internal part-time or external coach are the most common starting points. Let the team decide.

Watch Out For

Common patterns that undermine Scrum — even when teams have good intentions.

Scrum-But

“We do Scrum, but we skip retros” / “…but the PO writes the tasks” / “…but we don’t have a DoD.”

SM as Project Manager

The Scrum Master serves the team — they don’t assign work, track status, or report up.

No Real Increment

If nothing is “Done” at Sprint’s end, there’s nothing to inspect. The feedback loop breaks.

Impediments Stay

If blockers are raised but never removed, trust erodes. Someone must own clearing the path.

What impediments exist for your team today? Who would own removing them?
3-5 min. "Scrum-But" is the most common anti-pattern: adopt the ceremonies, skip the hard parts. Ask the group to name real blockers they face today.

Where Does “Scrum” Come From?

“The ‘rugby approach’ — where a team tries to go the distance as a unit, passing the ball back and forth — may better serve today’s competitive requirements.”

— Takeuchi & Nonaka, “The New New Product Development Game” (Harvard Business Review, 1986)

From Paper to Practice

Jeff Sutherland and Ken Schwaber took this idea and created the Scrum framework in the early 1990s, first presented at OOPSLA in 1995.
